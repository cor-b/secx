Threat Actor Motivation

Why attackers attack. Five motivations, each with a different target profile and a real-world example, from nation-state election interference to teenagers chasing notoriety.

overview

Threat actor motivations are the reasons that drive an attacker to target an organization. Five motivations: geopolitical (nation-states disrupting/influencing), espionage (stealing sensitive info), financial (monetary gain), activism/hacktivism (advancing a cause), and notoriety (recognition, bragging rights).

the five motivations

Geopolitical
Nation-states disrupting or influencing another country's political/economic stability, often via APTs. Example: Fancy Bear (Russian APT) allegedly influencing US/French/German/Ukrainian elections. Note on APTs: originally nation-state-funded sophisticated groups; now "APT" often refers to the sophisticated TTPs themselves, not necessarily nation-state backing.
Espionage
Stealing sensitive data (IP, trade secrets, state intelligence) for competitive/strategic advantage; attackers stay hidden as APTs. Example: the SolarWinds breach (APT29/Cozy Bear) inserting the SUNBURST backdoor.
Financial
Monetary gain via ransomware, fraud, data theft, often organized crime. Example: DarkSide's Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack disrupting East Coast fuel supplies.
Activism (hacktivism)
Advancing a political/social cause by disrupting services or leaking info. Example: Anonymous supporting the 2011 Arab Spring protests in Tunisia.
Notoriety
Attention, recognition, status, often thrill-seekers or script kiddies using available tools. Example: LAPSUS$ breaching Microsoft and Nvidia with basic tactics (phishing, social engineering).
EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Five motivations: geopolitical (nation-state), espionage (steal data), financial (money), activism (cause), notoriety (recognition).
  • Geopolitical = Fancy Bear/elections; espionage = SolarWinds; financial = DarkSide/Colonial Pipeline; activism = Anonymous; notoriety = LAPSUS$.
  • Espionage and geopolitical actors often operate as stealthy, long-term APTs.
  • "APT" now describes sophisticated TTPs, not necessarily nation-state funding.
  • Notoriety attackers are often low-skill (script kiddies) but can still cause high-impact disruption.
decision rules
  • Scenario disrupts a rival nation's elections → geopolitical
  • Scenario quietly steals trade secrets long-term → espionage
  • Scenario deploys ransomware for a payout → financial
  • Scenario defaces sites to promote a cause → activism/hacktivism
  • Scenario breaches a big name for bragging rights → notoriety
