Immediate Response

The first moments after detection: containing the threat technically while managing the communication around it.

overview

Immediate response is the rapid action taken to contain and mitigate an incident the moment it is detected. It has two parallel tracks: crisis management (coordinating communication with stakeholders to maintain trust and protect reputation) and threat response (the structured, technical work of detecting, containing, and eradicating the threat, often led by a CSIRT following NIST SP 800-61).

track 01

Crisis Management

Coordinated communication with stakeholders. Pre-planned comms order, out-of-band channels, reputation and recovery focus.

track 02

Threat Response

Structured technical handling: detect, contain, eradicate, recover. Led by a CSIRT, following the NIST SP 800-61 lifecycle.

crisis management

Crisis management handles the organizational side of a significant incident, the impact on operations and reputation, whereas threat response handles the active threat itself. Its emphasis is broad communication and recovery to keep stakeholders informed and maintain trust.

  • Pre-established communication plan: who is notified, in what order, what they receive, and how it is delivered
  • Order matters: in an IT incident the CISO or CIO is often the first call, not the CEO, so they can brief the executive level while the team handles the crisis
  • Out-of-band communication: encrypted messaging apps or dedicated phone lines keep internal discussions away from an attacker who may be in the primary channels
  • External messaging: coordinated statements to customers, partners, and media maintain transparency while avoiding panic

For a DDoS outage, the crisis team first alerts the CIO/CISO to scope the disruption, then prepares a customer statement explaining the outage, all over secure channels.

threat response

Threat response is the structured approach to identifying, addressing, and managing a specific threat, from detection through recovery and review. A cybersecurity incident response team (CSIRT) typically leads it with specialized tools (threat intelligence platforms, monitoring systems). It follows the four phases of NIST SP 800-61:

  1. Preparation — put tools, policies, and trained staff in place before an incident
  2. Detection & analysis — identify suspicious activity, confirm it is an incident, and assess severity and scope
  3. Containment, eradication & recovery — isolate affected systems, remove the threat, then restore from clean backups/patches
  4. Post-incident activity — root cause analysis, document lessons learned, update controls and training

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Immediate response = contain + mitigate right after detection, on two tracks: crisis management (communication) and threat response (technical).
  • Crisis management uses a pre-planned comms order (CISO/CIO often first, not the CEO) and out-of-band channels so an attacker in your network can't read the response.
  • Threat response is led by a CSIRT and follows NIST SP 800-61's four phases.
  • NIST SP 800-61 phases: preparation → detection & analysis → containment/eradication/recovery → post-incident activity.
  • Out-of-band communication is the term for keeping IR comms off potentially compromised primary channels.
decision rules
  • Scenario involves keeping customers and executives informed during a breach → crisis management
  • Scenario involves using encrypted apps so attackers can't read response comms → out-of-band communication
  • Scenario involves a CSIRT detecting, containing, and eradicating a threat → threat response
  • Question references the four-phase incident handling lifecycle → NIST SP 800-61
