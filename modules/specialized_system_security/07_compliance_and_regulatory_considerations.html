Compliance and Regulatory Considerations

Three lenses on staying within the rules: legal/industry regulations, the physical environment a system runs in, and the safety standards that protect people from harm.

overview

Compliance and regulatory considerations mean adhering to laws, standards, and guidelines so systems operate safely, securely, and legally. Three areas: regulatory (legal/industry standards like GDPR, HIPAA), environmental (the physical conditions a system needs: temperature, humidity, EMI), and safety (designing/operating to protect people, e.g. ISO 26262, IEC 61508).

area 01

Regulatory

Legal/industry standards (HIPAA, GDPR, FDA). Avoid fines, build trust, competitive advantage. Overseen by bodies like OCR, FDA.

area 02

Environmental

Physical conditions: temperature (64-81°F), humidity (40-60%), EMI. Data centers, clean rooms for semiconductors.

area 03

Safety

Protect users/public from harm via standards: IEC 61508 (functional safety), ISO 26262 (automotive).

regulatory considerations

Regulatory considerations ensure systems operate according to industry laws, standards, and guidelines, promoting legal, safe operations in healthcare, finance, and manufacturing. A healthcare company must comply with HIPAA to protect patient data; oversight comes from bodies like the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) (HIPAA privacy) and the FDA (medical devices). Compliance also builds trust, demonstrating commitment to privacy (e.g. GDPR in the EU) and supporting business continuity and risk management. Failure brings fines, legal battles, and reputational damage; compliance can become a competitive advantage attracting clients and partners.

environmental considerations

Environmental considerations ensure systems operate safely within their physical space. Data centers, sensitive to conditions, typically maintain temperatures of 64-81°F and humidity of 40-60% to prevent overheating, condensation, and electrostatic discharge. Electromagnetic interference (EMI) from nearby equipment or faulty wiring can disrupt data transmission. Neglecting these factors causes failures, downtime, data loss, and penalties. A strong example is clean rooms for semiconductor production, designed to limit dust and control humidity precisely because semiconductors are highly sensitive to particles and temperature.

safety considerations

Safety considerations ensure systems are designed and operated to protect operators, users, and the public. They follow standards like IEC 61508 (functional safety in electronic systems, including fail-safes and risk reduction). The automotive industry is a key example: cars must meet ISO 26262 (functional safety for automotive electrical/electronic systems), covering airbags and anti-lock braking so they perform reliably in emergencies. Meeting these standards prevents accidents, reduces liability, and maintains reliability.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Three areas: regulatory (laws/standards), environmental (physical conditions), safety (protecting people).
  • Regulatory: HIPAA (healthcare, OCR enforces), GDPR (EU data), FDA (medical devices).
  • Environmental: data centers 64-81°F, 40-60% humidity, watch for EMI; clean rooms for semiconductors.
  • Safety standards: IEC 61508 (general functional safety), ISO 26262 (automotive functional safety).
  • Don't confuse the two safety standards: ISO 26262 = cars; IEC 61508 = general/industrial.
decision rules
  • Scenario protects patient data under law → regulatory (HIPAA)
  • Scenario controls temperature/humidity for servers → environmental
  • Scenario certifies a car's braking system → safety (ISO 26262)
  • Scenario applies functional safety to an industrial system → safety (IEC 61508)
