Operational Technology (OT)

The hardware and software that runs the physical world: climate systems, factory machinery, and the geographically spread infrastructure of power and water. Availability comes first.

overview

Operational technology is the hardware/software that monitors and controls physical processes in critical infrastructure, prioritizing availability and integrity over confidentiality. Three examples: HVAC (climate control for people and equipment), ICS (industrial control systems automating machinery), and SCADA (a subset of ICS for remote monitoring across wide areas).

OT 01

HVAC

Climate control (temp, airflow, air quality) via sensors + PLCs, run from an HMI. Protects people and servers (data center cooling).

OT 02

ICS

Automates machinery via PLCs (ladder logic) + sensors + HMI. Interconnected ICS = DCS. Logs to a data historian.

OT 03

SCADA

ICS subset for remote control across wide areas (power grids, water). Connects field devices over cellular/satellite/VPN. Smart meters.

HVAC

HVAC is OT that controls a building's climate, temperature, airflow, and air quality, important for people's comfort/safety and for protecting sensitive equipment like data-center servers from overheating. It uses embedded sensors and controllers to adjust automatically. A core component is the programmable logic controller (PLC), programmed to act on sensor inputs (room too hot → activate AC). Operators monitor/control it via a human-machine interface (HMI), a panel or touchscreen. Like other OT, HVAC prioritizes availability: it must run continuously.

industrial control systems (ICS)

ICS automates and manages machinery through embedded devices, controlling physical processes (opening valves, generating electricity) in power, water, healthcare, and telecom. ICS prioritizes availability and integrity over confidentiality, the goal is keeping processes running. Key elements:

  • PLCs programmed in ladder logic, automating tasks from sensor input.
  • Fieldbus communication links PLCs with sensors and I/O devices.
  • HMI for operator interaction.
  • Interconnected ICS form a distributed control system (DCS) for large-scale operations.
  • A data historian collects and catalogs data for monitoring, troubleshooting, and security.

Example: on a US Navy warship, ICS manages power generation and water treatment so the ship runs as a self-contained city.

SCADA (Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition)

SCADA is a subset of ICS for remote monitoring and control across wide geographic areas, unlike standard ICS which controls a single location. It manages multiple ICS over distances (energy, water, telecom) using cellular, satellite, or VPN to connect remote field devices to a central control center. Embedded PLCs and sensors gather data and send it to a host computer for analysis, letting operators manage many sites and respond quickly. A common example: smart meters connecting to a central SCADA system via cellular to monitor energy usage, detect outages, and manage billing remotely.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • OT prioritizes availability and integrity over confidentiality (opposite of typical IT's CIA emphasis).
  • PLC = the controller acting on sensor inputs (ladder logic); HMI = the operator's control panel/screen.
  • ICS automates machinery in one facility; interconnected ICS = a DCS; a data historian logs ICS data.
  • SCADA = ICS subset for remote control across wide areas (power grids, water); smart meters are the classic example.
  • HVAC protects both people and equipment (data-center cooling) and must run continuously.
decision rules
  • Scenario remotely monitors a power grid across a region → SCADA
  • Scenario automates factory machinery in one plant → ICS
  • Scenario uses a control panel to run the system → HMI
  • Scenario programs a controller to act on sensor data → PLC (ladder logic)
