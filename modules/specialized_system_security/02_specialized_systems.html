Specialized Systems

Purpose-built technology with security baked in by design: an entire computer on one chip, dedicated embedded controllers, internet-connected devices, and the wireless that ties them together.

overview

Specialized systems are unique, purpose-built technologies, each needing tailored security. Four types: system-on-a-chip (SoC) (a whole computer on one chip, secure at the hardware level), embedded systems (dedicated-function controllers in larger devices), IoT devices (internet-connected, sensor-driven), and wireless technologies/RF (the radio links connecting them).

type 01

System-on-a-Chip

Whole computer (CPU, memory, GPU, radios) on one chip. Small, low-power, powerful. Raspberry Pi, Fire TV Stick. Secure at hardware level.

type 02

Embedded Systems

Dedicated-function computing inside larger devices (thermostat, engine control). Limited resources, prioritize reliability.

type 03

IoT Devices

Internet-connected, sensor-driven devices (smart lights, cameras). Convenient but expanded attack surface.

type 04

Wireless / RF

Radio links (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Zigbee, Z-Wave) connecting devices. Need strong encryption.

system-on-a-chip (SoC)

An SoC is an integrated circuit holding all the components of a computer, processor, memory, storage, GPU, and peripherals like USB controllers, power management, and Wi-Fi/Bluetooth radios, on a single chip. SoCs deliver high performance in a small, low-power form factor (a Raspberry Pi, a Roku/Fire TV Stick). Because everything is on one chip, security must be embedded at the hardware level to protect against physical and cyber threats.

embedded systems

Embedded systems are computing systems built into larger devices to perform a specific function, found in washing machines, medical devices, car engine control, and smart thermostats. Unlike general-purpose computers, they're dedicated to one job and run on limited resources (often a microcontroller or SoC), prioritizing reliability and efficiency. They take input, process it by defined rules, and produce output (adjusting temperature, controlling engine performance), which makes them cheaper and more efficient than a full OS for the task.

internet of things (IoT)

IoT is the global network of internet-connected devices that collect, send, and receive data using sensors and software. They range from smart-home gear (lights, doorbells, cameras, thermostats controllable by phone or voice) to industrial and healthcare equipment. In industry, IoT sensors monitor machinery and predict maintenance, reducing downtime. Their defining trait, pervasive connectivity, also makes them a significant security challenge.

wireless technologies (RF)

Wireless tech lets devices communicate without physical connections, using radio waves. RF (radio frequency) transmits data via electromagnetic waves (Wi-Fi routers, Bluetooth headphones/smartwatches). Two home-automation protocols compared:

Zigbee
Higher frequency (2.4 GHz): faster data transfer, supports more devices, good for bigger systems. But can face interference from other 2.4 GHz devices like Wi-Fi.
Z-Wave
Lower frequency (~900 MHz): better range, penetrates walls better, more reliable in larger homes. Less prone to 2.4 GHz interference.
EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • SoC = entire computer on one chip; secure at the hardware level (Raspberry Pi, streaming sticks).
  • Embedded systems = dedicated-function computing inside a larger device, limited resources, reliability-focused.
  • IoT = internet-connected sensor devices; pervasive connectivity expands the attack surface.
  • RF carries wireless data; Zigbee = 2.4 GHz (faster, more devices, more interference), Z-Wave = ~900 MHz (better range/wall penetration).
decision rules
  • Scenario puts a whole computer on one chip → system-on-a-chip
  • Scenario controls one function inside a device with minimal resources → embedded system
  • Scenario needs longer range and wall penetration at low power → Z-Wave
  • Scenario supports many devices with faster transfer at 2.4 GHz → Zigbee
