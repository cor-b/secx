Post-Quantum Implications

The families of quantum-resistant algorithms being built, and emerging approaches like code-based crypto and quantum key distribution.

overview

Preparing for quantum means two things: resistance to quantum decryption attacks (algorithms built on problems quantum computers struggle with, lattice-based, hash-based, multivariate polynomial) and emerging implementations (code-based cryptography and quantum key distribution) being tested for the quantum era.

area 01

Quantum-Resistant Algorithms

Lattice-based, hash-based, and multivariate polynomial cryptography, each posing math problems quantum computers can't solve efficiently.

area 02

Emerging Implementations

Code-based cryptography (McEliece) and quantum key distribution (QKD), gaining traction for quantum-era security.

resistance to quantum decryption attacks

Lattice-based cryptography
Security from the difficulty of problems in high-dimensional grids (lattices). Imagine a grid extending across hundreds or thousands of dimensions, quantum algorithms don't reduce it to a form they solve well. The leading PQC approach; supports encryption, signatures, and key exchange.
Hash-based cryptography
Security from the difficulty of reversing cryptographic hash functions. Creating a hash is easy; reversing it or finding a collision is computationally intensive even for quantum computers. Used for quantum-resistant digital signatures.
Multivariate polynomial cryptography
Security from the difficulty of solving systems of nonlinear equations with many variables. Easy to create, extremely hard to reverse without the key, like a riddle where every answer depends on many others. A different problem class quantum computers don't align with well.

emerging implementations

Code-based cryptography
Security from the difficulty of decoding random linear codes. The McEliece cryptosystem (decades old) is getting renewed attention for its quantum resistance, suited to large-scale systems like cloud storage. Drawback: it needs larger key sizes than traditional methods.
Quantum key distribution (QKD)
Uses quantum mechanics (qubits) rather than math to share a key securely. Qubits can't be observed or copied without altering their state, so any eavesdropping is detectable, making QKD theoretically immune to quantum attacks. Being tested in finance and government communications.

Note the contrast: the quantum-resistant algorithms are classical math designed to resist quantum attacks, while QKD uses quantum physics itself to secure the key exchange.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Quantum-resistant families: lattice-based (leading approach), hash-based (signatures), multivariate polynomial (nonlinear equations).
  • Lattice-based is the most prominent PQC approach for encryption, signatures, and key exchange.
  • Code-based cryptography = McEliece, strong resistance but large keys.
  • Quantum key distribution (QKD) uses qubits; eavesdropping alters their state and is detectable, theoretically immune to quantum attacks.
  • PQC algorithms are classical math resistant to quantum attacks; QKD uses quantum physics directly.
decision rules
  • Scenario adopts the leading quantum-resistant algorithm family → lattice-based cryptography
  • Scenario builds quantum-safe signatures on hash difficulty → hash-based cryptography
  • Scenario references the McEliece cryptosystem → code-based cryptography
  • Scenario detects eavesdropping because observing qubits changes them → quantum key distribution
