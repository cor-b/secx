Legal and Privacy Risks

Two intertwined risks: AI used in ways that discriminate or violate privacy, made worse when the model is a black box no one can explain or challenge.

overview

Legal and privacy rules ensure AI complies with laws to protect individual rights and personal data. Two implications: potential misuse (AI used in ways that cause discrimination, privacy violations, or harm, often on biased data) and explainable vs non-explainable models (explainable models give clear reasons for decisions; non-explainable "black box" models can't be easily interpreted, raising legal challenges where transparency is required).

risk 01

Potential Misuse

AI on biased data causing discrimination/privacy harm: predictive policing over-targeting communities, healthcare misdiagnosis from non-diverse data.

risk 02

Explainable AI

Clear, understandable reasons for decisions (e.g. a decision tree). Meets legal transparency, builds trust, allows recourse.

risk 03

Non-Explainable AI

"Black box" (deep learning): hard to interpret. Conflicts with transparency laws; can hide bias in high-stakes decisions.

potential misuse

Misuse arises when AI systems lead to discrimination, privacy breaches, or harm, often exacerbated by biased training data. In predictive policing, algorithms trained on biased historical crime data may disproportionately target certain communities, reinforcing bias and causing over-policing. In healthcare, a model trained on non-diverse data may perform poorly for under-represented demographics, risking misdiagnosis or inadequate care. These cases show the need for strict oversight and regulatory standards requiring diverse, representative data to protect rights and prevent harm.

explainable vs non-explainable models

Explainable models
Provide clear, understandable reasons for decisions, you can follow the logic. A decision tree in loan approval can show how income or credit history influenced the outcome. Increases trust, helps meet legal fairness requirements, and gives affected people a way to understand and challenge decisions.
Non-explainable models (black box)
Complex models (many deep-learning algorithms) whose decision process is hard to interpret. Can hide unintended bias; in credit scoring, a black-box model might deny loans on patterns reflecting historical discrimination, with no way for the person to understand or contest, risking anti-discrimination legal issues.

The tension: non-explainable models can be more accurate but conflict with transparency requirements, so regulated sectors (hiring, healthcare, lending) often favor explainable AI. Balancing accuracy and interpretability is a central issue in AI governance.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • AI misuse often stems from biased training data → discrimination/privacy harm (predictive policing, healthcare).
  • Explainable models give clear reasons for decisions (decision trees); they meet transparency requirements and allow recourse.
  • Non-explainable = black box (deep learning): hard to interpret, can hide bias, conflicts with transparency laws.
  • Regulated high-stakes domains (lending, hiring, healthcare) favor explainable AI.
  • The accuracy-vs-transparency trade-off is the central governance tension.
decision rules
  • Scenario can't explain why a loan was denied → non-explainable (black box) model
  • Scenario shows exactly which factors drove a decision → explainable model
  • Scenario over-targets a community from biased data → potential misuse
  • Scenario needs legal transparency for decisions → use explainable AI
