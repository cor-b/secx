AI Bots

Keeping automated digital workers safe and honest: limit what they can touch, fence in what they can do, stop them leaking data, and tell users they're talking to a bot.

overview

AI bots are automated programs that interact with users or systems to perform tasks (assistants, digital workers). Four concepts keep them safe: access and permissions (limit what data/systems they reach), guardrails (boundaries on their actions), data loss prevention (DLP) (stop sensitive data leaking), and disclosure of AI usage (tell users they're talking to a bot).

control 01

Access & Permissions

Limit what data/systems the bot can reach (a health bot sees general info, not patient records). Prevents overreach.

control 02

Guardrails

Boundaries on actions (a recommendation bot won't suggest out-of-stock items; a cleaning bot won't hit customers).

control 03

Data Loss Prevention

Encryption/restricted access so bots don't leak sensitive data (a banking bot won't share financials without verification).

control 04

Disclosure of AI Usage

Tell users they're interacting with a bot, not a human. Transparency and trust (especially in healthcare).

access and permissions

Access and permissions control what a bot can see or do, setting explicit limits so it only reaches the data and systems it needs. A healthcare patient-assistance bot may access general medical info but not specific patient records; a banking bot needs tightly controlled access to prevent unauthorized transactions. This keeps the bot "in its lane," protecting data integrity and privacy.

guardrails

Guardrails are predefined boundaries keeping a bot within intended limits, preventing it from veering into unintended territory or harmful autonomous decisions. An online-shopping recommendation bot has guardrails not to suggest out-of-stock products; a physical cleaning bot in a store has guardrails to avoid colliding with customers or merchandise and to reroute around obstacles. They keep bots helpful, accurate, and safe.

data loss prevention (DLP)

DLP measures (encryption, restricted-access policies) prevent bots from exposing sensitive data. A banking chatbot uses DLP to restrict sharing financial details without verification; an HR bot uses DLP so it can't share SSNs, salaries, or medical-leave records without identity verification. DLP keeps the bot within strict data-handling boundaries.

disclosure of AI usage

Disclosure means informing users they're interacting with a bot, not a human, essential for transparency and trust. A retail chatbot might open with "I am an AI assistant, here to help with product inquiries." In healthcare, patients should be clearly told they're talking to an AI, not a licensed professional, so they set appropriate expectations and approach responses with the right caution.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Four AI-bot controls: access/permissions, guardrails, DLP, disclosure of AI usage.
  • Access/permissions = limit what the bot can reach; guardrails = limit what it can do.
  • DLP = prevent sensitive data leaks (encryption, restricted access, verification).
  • Disclosure = transparency: tell users they're talking to a bot, important in healthcare.
  • Distinguish access/permissions (data scope) from guardrails (action boundaries).
decision rules
  • Scenario restricts which records a bot can read → access and permissions
  • Scenario stops a bot from suggesting out-of-stock items → guardrails
  • Scenario blocks a bot from sharing SSNs without verification → DLP
  • Scenario announces "I am an AI assistant" → disclosure of AI usage
