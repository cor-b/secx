Threats to the Model

Seven attack paths against an AI model itself: manipulating its inputs and outputs, poisoning its training, exhausting it, compromising its supply chain, stealing it, and reverse-engineering its data.

overview

Threats to the model compromise the integrity, security, or confidentiality of AI systems. Seven threats: prompt injection, unsecured output handling, training data poisoning, model denial of service, supply chain vulnerabilities, model theft, and model inversion. (These mirror the OWASP Top 10 for LLMs.)

the seven threats

Prompt injection
Manipulating input prompts to alter the model's behavior (tricking a chatbot into revealing confidential info or giving harmful advice). Defend with prompt filtering and model validation.
Unsecured output handling
Improper management of AI-generated outputs, leading to information leaks or execution of harmful commands (outputs exposing personal data). Defend with secure protocols and output monitoring.
Training data poisoning
Intentionally manipulating training data to skew predictions (biasing a content-moderation model to approve harmful content). Defend by auditing and securing training data.
Model denial of service
Overwhelming the AI with excessive requests so legitimate users can't access it (flooding an image-recognition tool). Defend with rate limiting and monitoring.
Supply chain vulnerabilities
Compromised third-party components/libraries or untrusted pre-trained models embedding malicious code. Defend by vetting and verifying third-party components.
Model theft
Unauthorized extraction of a model's intellectual property (repeatedly querying to replicate it). Defend with encryption and access controls.
Model inversion
Reconstructing sensitive training data by analyzing model outputs (reverse-engineering user demographics). Defend with differential privacy (adding calibrated noise).

two confusables

Watch the distinction between the two privacy threats: model theft extracts the model itself (its IP), while model inversion extracts the training data from the model's outputs. And prompt injection manipulates the input, while unsecured output handling mishandles the output.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Prompt injection = manipulate input; unsecured output handling = mishandle output.
  • Training data poisoning = corrupt the training set to skew predictions/bias.
  • Model theft = steal the model/IP (replicate it); model inversion = reconstruct training data from outputs.
  • Model DoS = flood with requests; supply chain = compromised third-party components/pre-trained models.
  • Differential privacy (calibrated noise) defends against model inversion.
decision rules
  • Scenario crafts an input to make a chatbot leak secrets → prompt injection
  • Scenario poisons the training set to bias outputs → training data poisoning
  • Scenario queries a model repeatedly to clone it → model theft
  • Scenario reconstructs training data from outputs → model inversion
