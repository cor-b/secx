AI Usage Risks

The dangers of using AI, even without an attacker: trusting it too much, feeding it secrets, letting it leak secrets, and handing it too much control.

overview

AI usage risks are dangers from implementing and relying on AI without proper controls. Four risks: overreliance (undervaluing human judgment), sensitive information disclosure to the model (feeding it private data without safeguards), sensitive information disclosure from the model (it leaking data it learned in training), and excessive agency (giving it too much autonomy).

risk 01

Overreliance

Trusting AI output without verification, undervaluing human judgment, context, and ethics. Can amplify training-data bias.

risk 02

Disclosure TO Model

Inputting private/confidential data without encryption, anonymization, or access controls. Compliance/breach risk.

risk 03

Disclosure FROM Model

Model unintentionally reveals data learned in training (a past customer's balance). Use differential privacy.

risk 04

Excessive Agency

Too much autonomy/control without human oversight (self-driving car in an untrained situation). Use human-in-the-loop.

overreliance

Overreliance is placing too much trust in AI output without verifying accuracy or considering context, ethics, training-data limits, or external factors. An analyst over-depending on an AI investment recommendation may overlook economic context or market anomalies, leading to loss. Overreliance can also amplify pre-existing data biases, since models trained on historical data reflect past biases. The fix is combining AI insights with human expertise.

sensitive information disclosure to the model

This is feeding private, confidential, or proprietary data into the AI without robust privacy controls (no encryption, anonymization, or role-based access). The data becomes part of the model's accessible pool, risking improper storage, unauthorized access, or unintended use, e.g. a customer-service AI receiving financial/health data without safeguards, creating compliance risk under GDPR/HIPAA. Mitigate with data anonymization, secure access controls, and clear data-usage guidelines.

sensitive information disclosure from the model

This is the model unintentionally releasing private data it learned during training from the model. An AI assistant trained on historical chats might reveal a past customer's account balance or transactions to a new user; in healthcare/legal contexts it might expose protected health info or case details, breaching confidentiality (HIPAA, attorney-client privilege). Mitigate with differential privacy and strict data-handling protocols that anonymize sensitive details from responses.

excessive agency

Excessive agency is giving an AI too much autonomy to act without human supervision, so it may operate beyond its intended scope. A self-driving car allowed to make all decisions could act dangerously in a situation it wasn't trained for. The fix is controlled autonomy: human-in-the-loop systems, structured workflows, decision auditing, and limits on AI decision-making power so humans review critical decisions.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Four usage risks: overreliance, disclosure TO the model, disclosure FROM the model, excessive agency.
  • Disclosure TO = you feed it secrets unsafely; disclosure FROM = it leaks what it learned in training. Watch the direction.
  • Overreliance undervalues human judgment and can amplify training-data bias; combine AI with human expertise.
  • Excessive agency = too much autonomy without oversight; fix with human-in-the-loop and decision auditing.
  • Differential privacy mitigates disclosure FROM the model.
decision rules
  • Scenario accepts AI output without verifying → overreliance
  • Scenario pastes confidential data into an AI tool → disclosure to the model
  • Scenario's model reveals another user's private data → disclosure from the model
  • Scenario lets AI act critically with no human review → excessive agency
