Hardware Assurance

Verifying that physical components are secure, reliable, and free from tampering through formal certification and continuous validation.

overview

Hardware assurance ensures physical components meet security standards across their full lifecycle. Two processes anchor it: certification, a formal evaluation before deployment, and validation, the ongoing testing that confirms components remain secure as updates and changes occur. Key frameworks include Common Criteria, the Trusted Foundry Program, and NIST publications 800-161 and 800-171.

process 01

Certification

Formal evaluation verifying hardware meets specific security standards before deployment. Performed against defined criteria by an independent evaluator.

process 02

Validation

Ongoing testing that confirms hardware continues to meet design and security standards over time, especially after updates, patches, or component changes.

certification

common criteria (CC)

Common Criteria is the internationally recognized framework for evaluating hardware (and software) security. It assesses how well a product defends against defined security threats. The depth of an evaluation is measured by its Evaluation Assurance Level (EAL), ranging from EAL 1 to EAL 7. Higher levels require more rigorous testing and are used for classified government and defense systems.

key documents in a CC evaluation

Protection Profile (PP)
Describes potential threats and required countermeasures for a class of product. Written independently of any specific product. For a chip, a PP might require protection against physical tampering, strong encryption to safeguard data, and a secure boot process. It defines what threats exist and what defenses must address them.
Security Target (ST)
Describes how a specific product meets the requirements defined in its Protection Profile. Product-specific. Maps the vendor's implementation choices (tamper-resistant packaging, AES-256, verified boot sequence, access controls on critical components) to the threats in the PP. Evaluated during certification to confirm the product fulfills its claims.

trusted foundry program

A U.S. Department of Defense initiative that certifies manufacturing facilities to produce critical hardware components securely. The program addresses the risk of malicious components or backdoors being inserted during manufacturing, which could compromise defense systems and sensitive government infrastructure.

certified facility requirements

  • Access control: only authorized personnel handle sensitive hardware at certified facilities
  • Supply chain security: components must be sourced from trusted, vetted suppliers
  • Data and communication security: sensitive information is protected through encryption throughout the manufacturing process
  • Regular audits and inspections: facilities undergo ongoing compliance checks to maintain certification status

Only Trusted Foundry-certified facilities are authorized to produce specific microelectronics and hardware components vital to national security.

validation

Validation is the ongoing counterpart to certification. Where certification is a formal gate, validation is continuous, ensuring hardware remains secure throughout its operational life, including after updates, changes, and integration into new environments.

NIST SP 800-161
Supply chain risk management for information and communication technology (ICT) systems. Provides guidance to federal agencies and contractors for identifying, assessing, and managing risks throughout the ICT supply chain. Emphasizes continuous validation with checks and controls at each stage of hardware development, manufacturing, and delivery.
NIST SP 800-171
Protecting Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) in non-federal systems and organizations. Sets guidelines for contractors working with the federal government. Outlines how to safeguard CUI during hardware development and across the hardware lifecycle, ensuring sensitive data is protected and hardware is capable of handling that data without compromise.
IoT Cybersecurity Improvement Act (2020)
Establishes cybersecurity requirements for IoT devices used by the federal government. Mandates that IoT devices, including their hardware components, meet specific security standards covering vulnerability management, patching, and authentication. Compliance must be validated from development through deployment and across the device's full operational lifecycle.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Certification is the formal, one-time evaluation; validation is the continuous process across the hardware lifecycle
  • Common Criteria EAL scale: EAL 1 = functionally tested (lowest), EAL 7 = formally verified (highest, used for classified/defense systems)
  • Protection Profile = describes threats and countermeasures for a product class; Security Target = maps a specific product's implementation to the PP
  • Trusted Foundry Program is a DoD initiative that certifies facilities to protect against backdoors or tampering during manufacturing
  • NIST 800-161 = supply chain risk management for ICT; NIST 800-171 = protecting CUI in non-federal systems
  • The IoT Cybersecurity Improvement Act (2020) applies to IoT devices procured and used by the federal government
decision rules
  • Scenario involves formally evaluating a product against security standards before deployment → certification (Common Criteria / EAL)
  • Scenario involves ongoing checks after updates or across a supply chain → validation (NIST 800-161)
  • Scenario involves a government contractor protecting sensitive data on hardware → NIST 800-171
  • Scenario involves securing the manufacturing process itself against tampering or backdoors → Trusted Foundry Program
  • Question asks about the depth or rigor of a CC evaluation → EAL level
