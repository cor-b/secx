Governance Roles & Responsibilities

Named roles that establish clear accountability for data, security, and compliance. Distinct from RACI (which assigns tasks). These define who plays what role across the organization.

overview

Beyond task-level RACI assignments, governance depends on named roles with defined authorities. The exam tests three categories: data roles (owner, custodian, steward), privacy/GDPR roles (controller, processor, subject), and organizational security roles (CISO, DPO, system owner). Confusing them is a common trap.

three role categories

DATA Owner / Custodian / Steward Internal organizational roles for managing data through its lifecycle.
PRIVACY Controller / Processor / Subject Legal roles defined by GDPR and most modern privacy laws.
SECURITY CISO / DPO / System Owner Organizational positions accountable for security and privacy programs.

Data Roles

Three internal organizational roles that together cover the full responsibility for data. The exam loves to test the distinction.

Data Owner
Accountable for the data. Makes decisions about classification, access policy, and risk acceptance. Usually a business leader (the VP of Marketing owns customer marketing data; the CFO owns financial data). Has authority but does not implement controls themselves.
Data Custodian
Responsible for technical implementation and day-to-day protection. Operates the storage systems, applies backups, enforces access controls, maintains encryption. Typically IT operations or database administrators. Executes the owner's decisions; does not set policy.
Data Steward
Responsible for data quality, meaning, and definitions. Ensures data is accurate, consistent, and usable. Defines what "active customer" means or how a duplicate record gets reconciled. Often a subject matter expert from the business side, working alongside both owner and custodian.

the most common trap

If a question asks who is accountable for a data-related decision (classify this dataset, approve access, accept risk), the answer is Data Owner, not the custodian. Custodians execute; owners decide.

Privacy & GDPR Roles

GDPR (and most modern privacy laws like CCPA, LGPD) define three legal roles. These describe legal relationships, not job titles.

Data Controller
Decides why and how personal data is processed. Bears primary legal responsibility under GDPR. Most companies are controllers for their own employee and customer data. The controller chooses the purpose, the scope, and the means of processing.
Data Processor
Processes personal data on behalf of the controller. Has fewer legal obligations than the controller, but must follow the controller's documented instructions. Cloud providers, payroll services, and analytics platforms are typical processors. Processors need a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with each controller.
Data Subject
The individual whose personal data is being processed. Has legal rights: access, rectification, erasure ("right to be forgotten"), portability, restriction of processing, objection. The data subject can exercise these rights against the controller, who must respond within defined timeframes (typically 30 days under GDPR).

Organizational Security Roles

Named positions accountable for portions of the security and privacy program. Some are required by law (DPO under GDPR for certain orgs); others are best practice.

CISO: Chief Information Security Officer
Sets enterprise security strategy. Owns the security program end to end: strategy, budget, risk appetite, incident response, board reporting. Reports to the CEO, CIO, or directly to the board in mature organizations. The single point of accountability for security.
DPO: Data Protection Officer
Mandatory under GDPR for certain organizations. Required for public authorities, organizations whose core activities involve large-scale processing of special category data, or large-scale monitoring of data subjects. The DPO must be independent, report to the highest level of management, and cannot be told what conclusions to reach. Cannot be the CISO or CIO (conflict of interest).
Privacy Officer
Owns the privacy program; typically the US-style equivalent of a DPO. Not legally mandated in the same way as a DPO, but standard practice for organizations handling significant personal data. Often a single person handles both roles in mid-size organizations.
System Owner
Accountable for a specific system's operation, security, and lifecycle. A narrower scope than the Data Owner. The System Owner is responsible for the system (the application, the server, the platform); the Data Owner is responsible for the data the system handles. The same person may hold both roles for some systems.
ISSO: Information Systems Security Officer
Federal and military equivalent of a security officer at the system level. Common in NIST 800-53 / FISMA / RMF contexts. Each system in a federal environment typically has a named ISSO accountable for that system's security posture.
Internal Auditor
Independent assessment of controls inside the organization. Reports to the audit committee of the board, not to the CISO or CIO, to preserve independence. Tests controls against policy and external requirements. Findings feed remediation plans.
External Auditor
Independent third-party assessment. Required for certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2), regulatory compliance (SOX, PCI-DSS), and many contracts. Provides credibility that internal teams alone cannot.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Data Owner = decides; Data Custodian = executes; Data Steward = data quality. The most-tested distinction in this lesson.
  • The Data Owner is a business leader, not someone in IT. IT operates as the custodian.
  • GDPR Controller decides why and how; Processor does the work. Controller bears primary legal responsibility.
  • Data Subjects have specific rights under GDPR: access, rectification, erasure, portability, restriction, objection.
  • DPO must be independent and report to highest management. Cannot be the CISO or CIO (conflict of interest).
  • DPO is mandatory under GDPR for public authorities and orgs doing large-scale processing of personal/special data.
  • Internal Auditor reports to the board's audit committee, not to security or IT, to preserve independence.
  • System Owner = a specific system; Data Owner = the data within (potentially many) systems. Different scopes.
quick decision rules
  • "Decides how data is classified" → Data Owner
  • "Implements encryption / backups / access controls" → Data Custodian
  • "Ensures data definitions are accurate" → Data Steward
  • "Decides purpose of personal data processing" → Data Controller (GDPR)
  • "Processes data on instructions from another company" → Data Processor (GDPR)
  • "Has the right to request erasure" → Data Subject (GDPR)
  • "Owns overall security strategy" → CISO
  • "Required by GDPR for certain orgs, must be independent" → DPO
  • "Independent assessment of internal controls" → Internal Auditor
  • "Third-party attestation for ISO 27001 / SOC 2" → External Auditor
