Governance Frameworks

Structured guidelines for aligning IT and security with business objectives. Three frameworks dominate: governance (COBIT), service management (ITIL), and cybersecurity outcomes (NIST CSF).

overview

Three frameworks own this space: COBIT (governance and management, the strategic layer), ITIL (IT service management, the operational layer), and NIST CSF (cybersecurity outcomes, the security risk layer). They are complementary, not competing.

the three frameworks

COBIT Governance & Management Aligns IT with business objectives. 5 domains, 40 objectives. The "what and why."
ITIL IT Service Management Optimizes how IT services are delivered. 34 practices. The "how."
NIST CSF Cybersecurity Framework Manages cyber risk through outcomes. 5 (or 6) functions, 23 categories. The "what good looks like."

COBIT

Control Objectives for Information and Related Technologies. A governance and management framework for enterprise IT, broader than security alone. Ensures IT supports business objectives while balancing benefit realization, risk optimization, and resource optimization.

5 domains

Split between one governance domain and four management domains:

governance (1 domain)

EDM: Evaluate, Direct, Monitor
The governance layer. Sets strategic direction, oversees performance, and ensures compliance. The "board and executive" domain.

management (4 domains)

APO: Align, Plan, Organize
Strategic planning and organization. Ensures IT strategy aligns with enterprise goals and resources are organized appropriately.
BAI: Build, Acquire, Implement
Delivery of new capabilities. Covers building, acquiring, and implementing solutions and changes.
DSS: Deliver, Service, Support
Day-to-day operations. Execution and support of IT services. Keeping things running.
MEA: Monitor, Evaluate, Assess
Ongoing performance monitoring. Tracks whether controls are working, evaluates performance, and assesses compliance.

Key numbers

  • 5 domains: 1 governance (EDM) + 4 management (APO, BAI, DSS, MEA)
  • 40 governance and management objectives, each providing practices, activities, metrics, and capability levels

ITIL

Information Technology Infrastructure Library. The world's most widely adopted IT service management framework. Provides best practices for delivering and managing IT services, used primarily by IT operations teams.

Key facts

  • 34 practices total. Information security management is one of them (integrated, not the focus).
  • Used primarily by IT operations teams
  • Standard in Fortune 500 and large enterprises
  • Works well with Agile, DevOps, and DevSecOps environments

NIST CSF

NIST Cybersecurity Framework. A voluntary, outcomes-based framework for managing cybersecurity risk. Originally published in 2014 (CSF 1.0), updated to 1.1 in 2018, and significantly expanded in 2024 to CSF 2.0 with the addition of a sixth function (Govern).

The 5 core functions (CSF 1.1)

  1. Identify Understand assets, business context, governance, and risk. You can't protect what you don't know about.
  2. Protect Safeguards to limit or contain a cybersecurity event. Access control, awareness training, data security, protective technology.
  3. Detect Identify the occurrence of a cybersecurity event. Continuous monitoring, anomalies, detection processes.
  4. Respond Take action regarding a detected event. Response planning, communications, analysis, mitigation, improvements.
  5. Recover Restore capabilities or services impaired by an event. Recovery planning, improvements, communications.
CSF 2.0 update

CSF 2.0 (2024) added a sixth function: Govern, placed at the center. It covers cybersecurity risk management strategy, expectations, roles & responsibilities, policy, and oversight. Govern wraps around all five operational functions.

If a question references CSF generally, the 5 functions (IPDRR) is the safe answer. If CSF 2.0 specifically, it's 6 (Govern + IPDRR).

Structure

  • Functions (5 or 6): highest level groupings
  • Categories (23): subdivisions of each function (e.g. Asset Management is a category under Identify)
  • Subcategories (~108): specific outcome statements that can be measured and audited

Implementation Tiers

Describe the degree to which an organization's cybersecurity risk management practices exhibit the characteristics defined in the framework. Not maturity levels, but progression of rigor.

Tier 1: Partial
Ad hoc, reactive. Risk management is informal; cybersecurity activities are not integrated.
Tier 2: Risk Informed
Risk management approved but not formalized organization-wide. Some processes documented.
Tier 3: Repeatable
Formal policies in place. Risk management practices are documented and applied consistently across the organization.
Tier 4: Adaptive
Continuous improvement based on lessons learned and predictive indicators. Cybersecurity is part of the culture.

Profiles

  • Current Profile: what cybersecurity outcomes are being achieved today
  • Target Profile: outcomes needed to meet risk management goals
  • The gap between Current and Target drives prioritization and roadmap

Who uses it

  • Voluntary for most private-sector orgs
  • Mandatory for federal contractors and agencies via Executive Order 14028
  • Often used as a common language between security teams, executives, and boards
  • Maps well to other frameworks (COBIT, ISO 27001, CIS Controls)
related: ISO 27001 / 27002

ISO 27001 is the international, certifiable Information Security Management System (ISMS) standard. Organizations get audited and certified against it.

ISO 27002 is the companion code of practice with the actual security controls (~93 controls in 4 themes as of the 2022 update, previously 114 controls in 14 domains).

Plays a similar role to NIST CSF for non-US orgs. Key difference: ISO 27001 is certifiable (third-party audit and certificate), CSF is not.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Memory hook: COBIT = Controls/Governance, ITIL = IT service management, NIST CSF = Cybersecurity outcomes.
  • NIST CSF 5 functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. Memorize the order (IPDRR).
  • CSF 2.0 added Govern as a sixth function in 2024. If unsure, the original 5 is the safe answer unless 2.0 is mentioned.
  • CSF Tiers (Partial / Risk Informed / Repeatable / Adaptive) describe rigor, not maturity. Don't confuse with CMMI levels.
  • CSF Profiles describe Current vs Target state. The gap drives the roadmap.
  • COBIT has 5 domains and 40 objectives. EDM is the only governance domain; APO/BAI/DSS/MEA are management.
  • ITIL has 34 practices. Security is one of them, not the focus.
  • ISO 27001 is the certifiable ISMS standard (international). ISO 27002 contains the actual controls.
quick decision rules
  • Aligning IT with business goals, broad governance → COBIT
  • IT service delivery, DevOps, operations teams → ITIL
  • Cybersecurity outcomes, common language for board → NIST CSF
  • "Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover" → NIST CSF (the 5 core functions)
  • "Evaluate, Direct, Monitor" → EDM (COBIT governance domain)
  • Certifiable security management standard → ISO 27001
  • Federal contractor cybersecurity requirement → NIST CSF (via EO 14028)
