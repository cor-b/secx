Communication & Reporting

Ensuring security information reaches the right people at the right time: a cornerstone of effective security program management.

overview

Security program management rests on two pillars: communication (sharing risk and incident information across teams and stakeholders) and reporting (documenting and distributing details for decisions and regulatory compliance). Failing at either compounds the damage of any security incident.

the two pillars

01 Communication Sharing risk, incident, and response information with internal teams and external stakeholders in a timely, coordinated way.
02 Reporting Documenting and distributing essential incident details to support decision-making and maintain regulatory compliance.

Communication

What it does

Communication ensures that critical information about risks, incidents, and responses flows efficiently throughout the organization and to relevant stakeholders. When done well, it enables rapid, coordinated action during security events.

Who needs to be informed

  • IT management: operational decision-making and resource allocation
  • Senior leadership: strategic decisions and external commitments
  • Affected employees: instructions to recognize, avoid, or contain threats
  • Public relations: preparation for external statements if needed
  • Regulators and affected parties: required disclosures depending on incident type

Example: phishing response

A financial institution detects a phishing campaign targeting employees. The incident response team immediately notifies IT management, senior leadership, and all affected employees through established channels. Employees receive clear instructions; management gets regular status updates on containment. The PR team prepares for a potential public statement. Result: swift containment, minimal damage, maintained stakeholder trust.

What breaks without it

  • Key decision makers remain uninformed, delaying response
  • Employees take no protective action because they don't know a threat exists
  • Uncoordinated teams work at cross-purposes
  • Affected individuals can't protect themselves in time

Reporting

What it does

Reporting ensures that essential details about security incidents, risks, and compliance status are documented and shared with relevant stakeholders. Accurate, timely reports let decision makers assess threats and maintain regulatory obligations.

Consequences of failure

  • Delayed organizational response to active threats
  • Vulnerabilities go unaddressed and unchecked
  • Regulatory violations (most jurisdictions require breach notification within a defined window)
  • Compounded legal and reputational damage

Example: Uber 2016

In 2016, attackers accessed personal information of approximately 57 million users, including 600,000 drivers in the United States. Rather than disclosing the breach, Uber allegedly paid the attackers $100,000 to delete the data and concealed the incident. The breach wasn't reported until 2017, nearly a year later. The cover-up resulted in a $148 million settlement with state attorneys general. Concealing the breach turned a serious incident into a far greater legal and reputational liability.

Real-World Failure Cases

Incident What failed Consequence
Equifax 2017 CVE-2017-5638 (Apache Struts) left unpatched for 4+ months; public notification delayed 6 weeks after discovery; key decision makers not promptly informed 147M records exposed; $575M settlement ($425M to affected persons)
Uber 2016 Breach concealed; attackers paid $100K to delete data; no disclosure for ~1 year 57M affected; $148M settlement with state attorneys general
CVE referenced
CVE-2017-5638 Apache Struts Remote code execution vulnerability in the Jakarta Multipart parser. Known and patchable. Equifax did not apply the patch for over four months.
exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Communication = sharing information with the right people to enable a coordinated response. Timely, clear, through established channels.
  • Reporting = documenting and distributing incident details for decision-making and regulatory compliance.
  • The Equifax failure was both: delayed internal communication (decision makers uninformed) and delayed public reporting (6 weeks after discovery).
  • The Uber failure was primarily a reporting failure. Actively concealing the breach is a violation in virtually every breach notification law.
  • Both failures resulted in settlements in the hundreds of millions, the cost of delayed or suppressed disclosure.
  • Prioritizing both communication and reporting minimizes damage, satisfies regulatory requirements, and maintains stakeholder trust.
quick decision rules
  • Who needs to know right now, to act? → Communication problem
  • Document for regulators / affected parties? → Reporting obligation
  • Equifax failure type → Both communication and reporting
  • Uber failure type → Reporting (active concealment)
  • Breach notification laws apply to → Reporting: timely disclosure to regulators and affected individuals
