Security Program Documentation

Four administrative controls that define what behavior is expected, how to carry it out, and to what standard. The backbone of a consistent security program.

overview

Policies, procedures, standards, and guidelines are all administrative controls; they regulate employee behavior and manage human and operational risk. Together they ensure consistent security practices aligned with business objectives and regulations.

the four document types

01 Policies High-level principles, mandatory with consequences for violations. Define the "what."
02 Procedures Step-by-step instructions for implementing policies, required to be followed. Define the "how."
03 Standards Specific requirements that must be met. Some carry penalties, others are best practices.
04 Guidelines Recommended but not required. Flexible best practices organizations can tailor.

Policies

High-level, mandatory principles that set expectations and define what is allowed. Violations carry consequences. Think of policies as the speed limits of security: you must follow them.

Key policy types to know

separation of duties

No single person controls all aspects of a critical process. Reduces fraud risk by requiring multiple people for sensitive actions.

  • Accounting: one person requests payment, a different person approves it
  • Security: one admin handles backups, another handles restoration

split knowledge

A variant of separation of duties: two individuals each hold half the information needed to complete a task.

  • Example: a cryptographic key split between two administrators, neither can act alone

job rotation

Multiple employees are trained on the same tasks and periodically swap roles.

  • Helps detect fraud (patterns become visible when someone new takes over)
  • Provides backup coverage and cross-training

mandatory vacation

Employees are required to take time off; duties are temporarily reassigned.

  • Can surface fraud or suspicious activity hidden by the absent employee
  • Also builds cross-training and backup readiness

Procedures

Detailed, step-by-step instructions for carrying out policies, required to be followed exactly. They eliminate ambiguity and reduce errors during critical moments.

Examples

Incident response procedure
Notify the IR team → document the breach → follow the communication plan → isolate affected systems → contact authorities
Backup and recovery procedure
Exact steps for performing backups and restoring data: who does what, in what order, using which tools

Standards

Define specific requirements that should be met. Some are contractually or legally mandatory; others are industry best practices without direct penalties.

Key standards to know

PCI-DSS
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard Contractual agreement, mandatory for any org handling credit card data. Noncompliance means losing the ability to process credit card transactions. Not a law, but enforced through contracts with card networks.
ISO 27000 series
International Organization for Standardization 60+ standards covering information system security management: IT networks, policies, and controls. Best practices, not legally binding.
CMMI (levels 1–5)
Capability Maturity Model Integration Process improvement model for software, products, and services. Higher level = more refined and optimized processes. Government contracts often require level 3, 4, or 5. Best practices, not legally binding.
NIST 800 series
National Institute of Standards and Technology 1,300+ standards across industries. NIST SP 800-53 = security and privacy controls for federal information systems: 800+ controls across 18 control areas for risk management. Best practices for most orgs; mandatory for federal agencies.

Guidelines

Recommended best practices, not required, flexible. Help organizations align with policies and standards on their own terms.

Examples

  • Recommended firewall rule configurations
  • Suggested encryption protocols
  • Password management advice: recommend MFA and complexity rules even when policy doesn't mandate them

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Policies and procedures are mandatory; violations have consequences.
  • Guidelines are never mandatory; always recommended.
  • Standards go either way: PCI-DSS has real penalties; ISO, NIST, and CMMI are best practices.
  • PCI-DSS is contractual (not a law); noncompliance means losing credit card processing ability.
  • Memorize the four policy examples: separation of duties, split knowledge, job rotation, mandatory vacation.
  • Driving analogy: Policy = speed limit, Procedure = turn-by-turn directions, Standard = stop signs, Guideline = Google Maps suggestion.
quick decision rules
  • Mandatory with consequences → Policy or Procedure
  • Step-by-step "how to" → Procedure
  • Contractually enforced security requirement → Standard (PCI-DSS)
  • Voluntary best practice → Guideline
  • No single person approves their own request → Separation of duties
  • Each person holds half the key → Split knowledge
