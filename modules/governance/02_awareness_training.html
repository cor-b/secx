Awareness & Training

Educating employees about security risks through initial and recurring training, building situational awareness and promoting operational security across the organization.

overview

The human layer is consistently the most exploited attack surface. Awareness and training programs address phishing, social engineering, physical security, and OPSEC, turning employees from a liability into a first line of defense. Simulations like the Social-Engineer Toolkit (SET) make training realistic and measurable.

Key Terms

Operational Security (OPSEC)
Protecting sensitive information from inadvertent exposure. Focuses on identifying critical data and controlling its dissemination. Asks: "What could an adversary learn from what we reveal?"
Physical Security
Protecting assets, facilities, and personnel from unauthorized access. Measures include surveillance, access control systems, and secure entry points. Employees must be trained to identify and respond to physical threats: tailgating, piggybacking, unauthorized visitors.
Privacy
Safeguarding personal and sensitive information from unauthorized access. Includes legal and regulatory compliance (GDPR, HIPAA, etc.). Distinct from OPSEC. OPSEC protects org-sensitive data from exposure; privacy protects individuals' personal data.
Social Engineering
Manipulation tactics used to trick individuals into divulging confidential information. Exploits human psychology, not technical vulnerabilities. Broad category. Phishing is one specific form.
Phishing
Fraudulent communications (typically email) designed to trick recipients. Goal: get the target to click a malicious link or reveal credentials. A specific, common form of social engineering.
Situational Awareness
Clear understanding of the current threat landscape. Constant vigilance to detect and respond to incidents as they arise. Not a technical control; it's a mental posture reinforced through training.

Social-Engineer Toolkit (SET)

An open-source tool used to simulate social engineering attacks for security awareness training. SET exposes employees to realistic attack scenarios, demonstrating how easily credentials can be harvested.

What SET can simulate

  • Phishing campaigns
  • Credential harvesting via cloned websites
  • Spearphishing (targeted) attack vectors
  • Mass mailer attacks
  • Website-based attack vectors

Credential harvester demo flow

  1. Launch SET Run sudo setoolkit to open the menu-driven interface.
  2. Select attack type Choose "Social Engineering Attacks" from the main menu.
  3. Choose vector Select "Website Attack Vectors."
  4. Select method Choose "Credential Harvester Attack Method."
  5. Pick a template Select a web template (Google, Twitter, or Java) to clone.
  6. Host the fake site SET serves the cloned page from the attacker's IP address.
  7. Victim enters credentials Credentials are captured in the attacker's terminal in real time.
  8. Redirect to real site Victim is silently forwarded to the legitimate site. They assume a failed login and try again. Attack is invisible.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Social engineering = broad category of human manipulation. Phishing = a specific, email-based form of social engineering.
  • OPSEC = protect sensitive org data from inadvertent disclosure. Privacy = protect individuals' personal data from unauthorized access. Related but distinct.
  • SET is a training and simulation tool. On the exam, recognize it as used for security awareness, not just as an attack tool.
  • Situational awareness = vigilance and recognition: a posture, not a technical control.
  • The credential harvester attack is invisible to the victim. They see what looks like a failed login then a redirect. This is the key teaching moment in SET demos.
quick decision rules
  • Human manipulation, no technical exploit → Social engineering
  • Fraudulent email with malicious link → Phishing
  • Controlling what sensitive info gets out → OPSEC
  • Protecting personal data per regulations → Privacy
  • Simulating phishing to train employees → SET (Social-Engineer Toolkit)
