Vendor & Third-Party Risk Management

Managing risk from vendors, suppliers, and service providers. The agreements that govern these relationships, the assessments that vet them, and the supply chain realities behind today's biggest breaches.

overview

Most organizations depend on dozens or hundreds of third parties. Recent breaches like SolarWinds (2020) and Log4j (2021) made supply chain risk a top governance concern. Effective third-party risk management combines clear agreements, ongoing assessment, and explicit ownership of the trust boundary.

Vendor Agreement Types

Each agreement type serves a different governance purpose. The exam tests both the acronyms and the situations where each applies.

SLA: Service Level Agreement
Defines specific service levels the vendor must meet. Includes uptime guarantees (e.g. 99.9% availability), response times, support tiers, penalties for non-compliance. The "what you'll get and when" document.
MSA: Master Service Agreement
Overarching contract covering the general terms of the relationship. Sets liability, IP ownership, confidentiality, dispute resolution. Individual statements of work (SOWs) hang off the MSA without renegotiating the whole contract each time.
MOU / MOA: Memorandum of Understanding / Agreement
Non-binding statement of intent between two parties. Documents that both sides agree on goals or expectations, but does not create legal obligations. Often used between government agencies or partner organizations before formal contracts.
NDA: Non-Disclosure Agreement
Confidentiality obligations between parties. Binds the vendor (and often their employees) to not disclose sensitive information. Can be one-way (vendor protects your data) or mutual (both parties protect each other's data).
BPA: Business Partner Agreement
Governs a strategic business partnership. Used for joint ventures, revenue-sharing deals, or co-marketing arrangements. Defines roles, contributions, profit splits, and exit conditions. Broader than an SLA.
ISA: Interconnection Security Agreement
Technical and security requirements for connecting two networks. Specifies authentication, encryption, logging, allowed traffic, and incident notification when one organization's network connects to another. Common between federal agencies and contractors.

Vendor Risk Assessment

Common assessment methods

  • Vendor security questionnaires like SIG (Standardized Information Gathering) or CAIQ (Consensus Assessment Initiative Questionnaire from CSA) standardize what you ask vendors
  • SOC 2 reports from an independent auditor demonstrating the vendor's control environment
  • Penetration test results if the vendor will share them under NDA
  • Right to audit clauses in the contract let you verify controls directly
  • Continuous monitoring via security ratings services (BitSight, SecurityScorecard) for ongoing visibility

SOC 2 Type I vs Type II

SOC 2 Type I
Point-in-time snapshot. Auditor confirms the controls are designed appropriately as of a specific date. Quicker to obtain but less rigorous; says nothing about whether controls actually operate over time.
SOC 2 Type II
Period-of-time evaluation (typically 6-12 months). Auditor tests whether controls operated effectively throughout the period. The stronger, more trusted report. If a vendor offers Type I only, ask when Type II will be available.

Risk tiering

Not every vendor needs the same scrutiny. Tier vendors by impact:

  • Critical: handles sensitive data or core operations (e.g. cloud hosting, payment processor). Annual deep assessment, contractual security requirements, continuous monitoring.
  • High: meaningful access or data exposure. Annual questionnaire, SOC 2 review.
  • Medium: limited access. Questionnaire on onboarding, periodic review.
  • Low: minimal risk (e.g. marketing newsletter tool). Light or no formal assessment.

Vendor Lifecycle

  1. Onboarding & Due Diligence Risk assessment before signing. Review questionnaires, SOC 2, financial stability, legal/compliance history. Negotiate security and audit clauses into the contract.
  2. Contracting Execute MSA, SLA, NDA, ISA, and any other applicable agreements. Document the trust boundary and incident notification requirements.
  3. Operations & Monitoring Track SLA performance, monitor security ratings, conduct periodic reassessments (annual for critical vendors), watch for breaches affecting the vendor.
  4. Offboarding Return or destroy data, revoke access credentials and API keys, obtain certificate of destruction for any media. Document the end of the relationship.

Supply Chain Risk

Vendor risk management covers the contractual relationship. Supply chain risk covers the deeper question: what's inside the products and services you depend on?

Software supply chain

  • Modern software is mostly third-party components. A breach in any component cascades downstream.
  • SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) lists all components in a piece of software. Required for federal contractors via Executive Order 14028.
  • Watch for transitive dependencies (your vendor's vendors' vendors).
  • Examples: SolarWinds (compromised build pipeline pushed malicious updates to ~18,000 customers), Log4j (a vulnerability in a ubiquitous logging library exposed thousands of products at once).

Hardware supply chain

  • Counterfeit components in network equipment, especially common in the gray market
  • Tampering during manufacturing or shipping (interdiction attacks)
  • Country-of-origin restrictions (e.g. US bans on Huawei, ZTE for critical infrastructure)
  • Mitigations: buy from authorized distributors, validate firmware signatures, tamper-evident packaging

Cloud and SaaS dependencies

  • 4th-party risk: your vendor's vendors. If your SaaS provider uses AWS and AWS has an outage, you're affected too.
  • Shared responsibility models define what the cloud provider secures vs what the customer secures. Misunderstanding this boundary causes most cloud breaches.
  • Concentration risk: if too many vendors depend on the same upstream provider, a single incident can affect a whole industry.
why SolarWinds & Log4j matter

SolarWinds (2020): attackers compromised the Orion software build pipeline. Trusted, signed updates carried a backdoor (SUNBURST) to ~18,000 customers including federal agencies and Fortune 500 companies. Demonstrated that the vendor's build environment is a target, not just their product.

Log4j / Log4Shell (2021): a critical RCE vulnerability (CVE-2021-44228) in a Java logging library used by tens of thousands of products. Most orgs didn't even know they had it (deep transitive dependency). Drove widespread adoption of SBOM requirements.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Know all six agreement acronyms: SLA, MSA, MOU, NDA, BPA, ISA. Each maps to a distinct situation.
  • SOC 2 Type II is stronger than Type I. Type I is point-in-time; Type II is period-of-time (typically 6-12 months).
  • SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) is required for federal contractors via Executive Order 14028.
  • Shared responsibility models in cloud define what the provider secures vs what the customer secures. Misunderstanding this is the #1 source of cloud breaches.
  • Right to audit clauses in vendor contracts let you verify controls; SOC 2 reports are the common alternative when audits aren't practical.
  • SolarWinds = supply chain attack via compromised build pipeline. Log4j = vulnerability in a deeply embedded open-source library. Both are go-to exam examples.
  • Tier vendors by risk: not every vendor needs the same scrutiny.
quick decision rules
  • Uptime guarantee, response time → SLA
  • Overarching contract terms → MSA
  • Non-binding statement of intent → MOU / MOA
  • Confidentiality only → NDA
  • Strategic partnership, joint venture → BPA
  • Connecting two networks securely → ISA
  • Point-in-time vendor control review → SOC 2 Type I
  • Period-of-time vendor control review → SOC 2 Type II
  • Inventory of software components → SBOM
  • Cloud provider vs customer security responsibility → Shared responsibility model
