Email Security

Four controls, best understood as a fancy party: SPF is the guest list, DKIM is the tamper-proof stamp, DMARC sets the rules for handling gate-crashers, and S/MIME seals the message itself.

overview

Email security protects against phishing, spoofing, and unauthorized access. Four concepts: SPF (lists which mail servers may send for a domain), DKIM (digital signature proving integrity and sender), DMARC (policy + reporting built on SPF/DKIM results), and S/MIME (encryption and signatures on individual messages). SPF, DKIM, and DMARC publish records in DNS.

control 01

SPF

The guest list: domain owner specifies authorized sending servers (DNS TXT record). Soft fail (~) or hard fail (-). Doesn't protect the From header.

control 02

DKIM

The tamper-proof stamp: digital signature (private key) verified via public key in DNS. Proves integrity and origin.

control 03

DMARC

The rulebook: policy (reject/quarantine/flag) when SPF/DKIM fail, aligns them with the From address, plus reporting.

control 04

S/MIME

Seals the message: per-message encryption + digital signature. Confidentiality, integrity, authentication, non-repudiation.

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

SPF prevents spoofing by letting a domain owner specify which mail servers may send on the domain's behalf, like a guest list at a party. The receiving server checks the sender domain's SPF record (a DNS TXT record listing authorized domains/IP ranges); mail from a server not on the list is flagged or rejected. Policy symbols set strictness: tilde (~) = soft fail, dash (-) = hard fail. SPF's limit: it doesn't stop forging the From header, and legitimate third-party senders (surveys, etc.) can fail it, so it's combined with DKIM and DMARC.

DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

DKIM attaches a digital signature to every outgoing email using the domain's private key; the recipient verifies it with the domain's public key (a DNS TXT record). A valid signature confirms the message wasn't altered in transit and truly came from the claimed sender, like a special stamp verifying a party invitation is authentic and untampered. DKIM's limit: it protects integrity but doesn't authenticate the From address, so an attacker could still forge it, which is why DKIM is paired with DMARC.

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance)

DMARC builds on SPF and DKIM, letting a domain owner publish a policy telling receiving servers what to do when checks fail: reject, quarantine, or flag the email. Crucially, it ensures SPF and DKIM align with the From address, closing the forged-header gap. It's the party rulebook for handling gate-crashers, and it adds reporting: domain owners get feedback on how their mail is handled, helping detect spoofing attempts and tune policy.

S/MIME (Secure/Multipurpose Internet Mail Extensions)

S/MIME secures non-text email (graphics, video) and applies encryption and digital signatures at the message level, providing confidentiality, integrity, authentication, and non-repudiation, using public-key cryptography. Unlike SSL/TLS (which secures the connection but not the stored message), S/MIME protects the individual email. The flow: parties exchange and validate public keys (via a certificate authority), then a unique symmetric session key encrypts each message, and that session key is encrypted with the recipient's public key. The recipient uses their private key to decrypt the session key, then the message. A drawback: because S/MIME encrypts the whole email, malware inside it can slip past filters that can't decrypt and inspect content.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • SPF = authorized sending servers (DNS record); ~ soft fail, - hard fail. Doesn't protect the From header.
  • DKIM = digital signature (private key sign, public key verify in DNS) proving integrity + origin.
  • DMARC = policy (reject/quarantine/flag) + reporting built on SPF/DKIM, aligns them with the From address.
  • S/MIME = per-message encryption + signature (confidentiality, integrity, authentication, non-repudiation) via session keys.
  • S/MIME drawback: encrypted malware can bypass filters that can't inspect the content.
decision rules
  • Scenario lists which servers may send for a domain → SPF
  • Scenario adds a signature proving the email wasn't altered → DKIM
  • Scenario sets what to do when SPF/DKIM fail and reports on it → DMARC
  • Scenario encrypts and signs an individual message end to end → S/MIME
