User Identity Control

Proving a user is who they say: what they know (credentials), what they are (biometrics), and stacking factors together with multi-factor authentication.

overview

User identity control manages, verifies, and secures user identities. Three concepts: credentials (info you provide, passwords/PINs/certificates), biometrics (unique physical/behavioral traits), and multi-factor authentication (MFA) (two or more independent factors). Authentication validates the presented credentials/biometrics; authorization then determines what the user may access.

method 01

Credentials

Something you know/have: passwords, PINs, digital certificates. Simple but vulnerable to guessing, theft, reuse; certificates are stronger (crypto).

method 02

Biometrics

Something you are: fingerprint, face, iris, voice. Hard to replicate; measured by CER, FAR, FRR.

method 03

MFA

Two or more factors: know, have, are, where. Far harder to defeat than single-factor.

credentials

Credentials are the info a user provides to prove identity. Passwords/PINs are the most basic and widely used, but their simplicity makes them vulnerable: weak passwords (123456, "password") are easily cracked, and even strong ones fail if reused or exposed in a breach. Digital certificates offer higher security, electronic documents that verify identity cryptographically, used for corporate network access and secure websites, authenticating users without relying on easily compromised secrets.

biometrics

Biometrics verify identity using unique physical or behavioral traits (fingerprint, face, iris, retina, voice), relying on something you are. They're convenient and hard to replicate, but imperfect, measured by three metrics:

CER (crossover error rate)
The point where false acceptance and false rejection rates are equal. A lower CER means a more accurate system, the key metric for comparing systems.
FAR (false acceptance rate)
How often the system wrongly accepts an unauthorized person. High FAR = not secure enough (lets the wrong people in).
FRR (false rejection rate)
How often the system wrongly rejects an authorized user. High FRR = inconvenient (locks out the right people).

multi-factor authentication (MFA)

MFA requires two or more independent factors, layering security so a single compromised factor isn't enough. The factors:

  • Something you know: password, PIN, security questions.
  • Something you have: smart card, key fob, smartphone receiving codes.
  • Something you are: biometrics (fingerprint, face, voice).
  • Somewhere you are: location data (GPS, IP address).

One factor = single-factor authentication. Two different factors = two-factor (2FA). Combining two or more distinct factors = MFA. Evolving methods include TOTP (time-based one-time password) and HOTP (HMAC-based one-time password), which generate single-use codes. MFA can be in band (code on the same device used for access) or out of band (code on a different device), with out-of-band adding an extra layer.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Factors: something you know, have, are, and where you are. Two distinct factors = 2FA; two or more = MFA.
  • Biometric metrics: CER (lower is better, equal-error point), FAR (wrongly accepts), FRR (wrongly rejects).
  • Digital certificates are stronger credentials than passwords (cryptographic, not guessable).
  • TOTP = time-based one-time password; HOTP = HMAC/counter-based one-time password.
  • Out-of-band MFA (code on a separate device) is more secure than in-band.
decision rules
  • Scenario compares biometric accuracy with one number → CER (lower is better)
  • Scenario lets an unauthorized person in → false acceptance (FAR)
  • Scenario locks out a legitimate user → false rejection (FRR)
  • Scenario uses password + smartphone code on a separate device → out-of-band MFA
