Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response (SOAR)

The platform that turns incident response from manual checklists into automated workflows: a playbook is the plan, a runbook is the plan running itself, pausing only when a human is needed.

overview

SOAR platforms streamline and automate security operations to detect, respond to, and mitigate threats efficiently, often integrating with SIEM. Two key concepts: playbooks (predefined workflows/checklists detailing the steps for a type of incident) and runbooks (the automated execution of a playbook, step by step, with pauses for human input). Modern SOAR adds machine learning/AI.

concept 01

Playbook

The plan: a predefined workflow/checklist of steps for a specific incident type (phishing, DDoS, malware). Human-readable procedure.

concept 02

Runbook

The plan executing itself: automation of a playbook, partially or fully, with checkpoints that pause for analyst confirmation.

playbooks

Playbooks outline the specific actions to take for different incident types, acting as a checklist guiding teams through detection and response. Prepared in advance as part of incident response documentation, they ensure a clear, standardized approach for emergencies. An analyst categorizes something suspicious as an incident, assigns it to a handler, who follows the appropriate playbook. Common dedicated playbooks: DDoS, malware, phishing, data exfiltration. Pre-made templates exist (e.g. the Incident Response Consortium's phishing playbook, based on the NIST process, with flowcharts for prepare/detect/analyze/contain/eradicate/recover); Microsoft also provides technical playbooks.

runbooks

A runbook is the automation of a playbook, executing the steps via a SOAR platform, partially or fully, with periodic checkpoints for human intervention. Example phishing response: a runbook automatically deletes the email from inboxes, checks who else received it, isolates workstations that opened it, runs virus and registry scans, and backs up data, then pauses for the analyst to confirm before re-imaging, then resumes to reconnect the workstation. Doing this manually would be slow and error-prone; runbooks increase efficiency and let one analyst handle far more work, freeing them for complex tasks. SOAR orchestrates playbooks and enriches data from SIEM, and modern platforms use ML/AI to refine detection and responses over time.

common playbooks/runbooks to implement

Ransomware
Determine impacted systems, infection method, isolate affected systems quickly; keep systems powered on to retain encryption keys in RAM for forensics.
Data exfiltration
Notification requirements, system and forensic analysis to determine what was accessed/exfiltrated (via SQL injection, password compromise, lateral movement); protect key data stores.
Social engineering (phishing)
Identify phishing emails and who interacted, reset passwords, re-image workstations, analyze the email in a sandbox for IOCs (IPs, URLs).
EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Playbook = the human-readable plan/checklist for an incident type; runbook = the automated execution of that playbook.
  • Runbooks pause at checkpoints for human confirmation (e.g. before re-imaging), then resume.
  • SOAR integrates with SIEM and increasingly uses ML/AI for detection and response.
  • Ransomware: isolate fast but keep systems powered on to preserve encryption keys in RAM.
  • Three common playbooks: ransomware, data exfiltration, social engineering/phishing.
decision rules
  • Scenario describes the documented steps for an incident type → playbook
  • Scenario auto-executes those steps with a pause for approval → runbook
  • Scenario keeps an infected machine powered on for key recovery → ransomware playbook
  • Scenario sandboxes a suspicious email to extract IOCs → phishing playbook
