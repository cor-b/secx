Privacy Regulations

Four major privacy laws that shape how organizations collect, store, and use personal data across different jurisdictions.

overview

Privacy regulations apply based on where your users are located, not where your business is headquartered. A US company serving EU residents must comply with GDPR. A non-Brazilian company processing data from Brazilian customers must comply with LGPD. Location of the data subject is the trigger, not the location of the business.

The four key regulations are COPPA (US, children under 13), LGPD (Brazil), CCPA (California), and GDPR (EU). Know each by its jurisdiction, its key threshold or trigger, and the rights it grants individuals.

the four regulations

US FEDERAL COPPA Children's Online Privacy Protection Act. Protects children under 13. Applies to any site that knowingly collects data from under-13s, even if children lied about their age to gain access. Enforced by the FTC.
BRAZIL LGPD General Data Protection Law. Applies to any company processing personal data from Brazilian residents, regardless of where the company is located. Extraterritorial scope modeled on GDPR principles.
US — CALIFORNIA CCPA California Consumer Privacy Act. Applies to businesses that meet specific revenue or data volume thresholds. Gives California residents the right to know, delete, and opt out of the sale of their personal data.
EUROPEAN UNION GDPR General Data Protection Regulation. Strongest privacy protections globally. Requires informed consent for data collection and gives EU residents the right to access, amend, or erase their data (right to be forgotten).

regulation details

COPPA

COPPA sets requirements for website operators and online services that collect personal information from children under 13. It applies to any site that knowingly collects such data, even if the site is not specifically targeting children and even if children lied about their age to gain access.

  • Parental consent must be obtained before collecting personal information from children under 13
  • Enforced by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC)
  • Fines up to $40,000 per violation, which can accumulate rapidly across individual incidents
  • Large companies (Facebook, Google) feel less financial impact than small businesses, making COPPA enforcement debated in practice

LGPD

Brazil's LGPD sets requirements for how organizations handle personal data belonging to individuals in Brazil. Location of the data subject is the trigger: even a company with no Brazilian presence must comply if it processes data from Brazilian residents.

  • Explicit consent required before collecting user data
  • Users have the right to access, correct, or delete their data
  • Fines up to 2% of company revenue in Brazil, capped at 50 million reals per violation

CCPA

CCPA gives California residents control over the personal data companies collect about them. It applies to businesses that meet at least one of two thresholds, regardless of where the business is located.

  • Applies to businesses earning over $25 million in annual revenue, or those handling personal information of 50,000 or more California customers
  • Individual rights: right to know what data is collected, right to request deletion, right to opt out of the sale of data to third parties
  • Fines up to $7,500 per intentional violation

GDPR

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation is the most comprehensive consumer privacy law in force. It is enforced by data protection authorities in each EU member state and applies to any organization processing personal data from EU residents.

  • Informed consent: data may only be collected and used for the specific purpose clearly disclosed at the time of collection, in plain language. Repurposing data (such as adding a marketing list) requires separate, explicit consent
  • Right to withdraw consent at any time
  • Right to inspect, amend, or erase data held about them, commonly called the right to be forgotten
  • GDPR protections apply to EU residents only. Non-EU individuals (such as Americans) are not legally protected and companies are not obligated to honor the same deletion rights for them

exam tips

key takeaways

  • All four regulations apply extraterritorially. Jurisdiction follows the data subject, not the company's headquarters. A US company with EU users must comply with GDPR.
  • COPPA applies even when children lie about their age. If a platform knows children under 13 are using it, COPPA requirements apply regardless of how those users registered.
  • GDPR = informed consent + right to be forgotten. These are the two most testable GDPR concepts. Informed consent means data is used only for the specific purpose disclosed at collection, in plain language.
  • CCPA threshold: $25 million annual revenue or 50,000+ California customers. Below both thresholds, CCPA does not apply.
  • LGPD is Brazil's GDPR equivalent. For the exam, treat LGPD as Brazil's extraterritorial data protection framework with similar individual rights.
quick identification
  • Children under 13, US, parental consent, FTC → COPPA
  • Brazil, any company processing Brazilian resident data → LGPD
  • California residents, $25M+ revenue threshold → CCPA
  • EU residents, informed consent, right to be forgotten → GDPR
