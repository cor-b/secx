Cross-Jurisdictional Compliance

Managing legal and regulatory obligations that span multiple countries, covering due diligence, due care, contractual requirements, legal holds, and export controls.

overview

Cross-jurisdictional compliance requires organizations to align their practices with multiple legal systems at once. The key principle: obligations are triggered by where your data goes and who your users are, not by where your headquarters is located.

Key areas: due diligence (investigation before action), due care (ongoing maintenance after action), contractual obligations (cross-border legal requirements), legal hold and e-discovery (evidence preservation), and export controls (dual-use technology restrictions).

due diligence and due care

Due diligence and due care are frequently tested together. The distinction is timing: diligence happens before an action, care happens continuously after it.

Due Diligence
All the preparation and investigation a company does before taking a significant action, such as adopting new software, onboarding a vendor, or launching a new service. Due diligence surfaces potential risks early so appropriate controls can be built in from the start. Analogy: researching a car before buying it by reading reviews, checking history, and comparing prices.
Due Care
The ongoing actions taken to maintain and protect against risks after the initial action is taken. Once a system is deployed, due care means actively keeping it secure: regular patching, continuous monitoring, and maintaining active controls. Analogy: regular oil changes and tire rotations after buying the car. You identified the risks before purchase; now you maintain the vehicle to prevent breakdowns.

contractual obligations

Contracts that span multiple jurisdictions must explicitly address varying legal requirements. Legal and regulatory standards differ widely across countries, and a single contract may need to satisfy obligations in several systems simultaneously.

  • A US company handling data for EU clients must comply with US regulations and GDPR simultaneously
  • Contracts should name which party is responsible for ensuring compliance with local law in each region where the contract operates
  • Failure to track varying privacy laws and data protection standards across jurisdictions creates significant legal exposure

legal hold and e-discovery

legal hold

A legal hold requires an organization to preserve certain information that may be relevant to legal proceedings. During a legal hold, data cannot be deleted or altered, even if it would normally be purged under a standard retention policy.

  • The preserved information may or may not ultimately be used in court
  • The case may not go to trial, or legal counsel may choose not to use the preserved evidence
  • Goal: retain everything potentially relevant for as long as the hold is active

e-discovery

E-discovery is the process of searching through preserved data (emails, documents, saved files) to identify what is relevant to a specific legal case. It follows a legal hold: first preserve, then search.

  • Conducted by legal teams (lawyers and paralegals), IT professionals, and sometimes specialized third-party e-discovery vendors
  • The scope of the search is defined by what is relevant to the particular case

export controls

Export controls regulate the international transfer of dual-use technologies: products and tools that have both civilian and military applications. Strong encryption is a classic example.

Wassenaar Arrangement

An international agreement among participating countries that controls the export of dual-use technologies, including many cybersecurity tools. Organizations must determine whether their software falls under this arrangement before transferring it internationally.

  • Strong encryption used in VPNs and firewalls qualifies as dual-use technology
  • Example: Check Point's Firewall and VPN software uses strong encryption, making it subject to the Wassenaar Arrangement
  • Exporting such software to certain countries may require an export license, especially to countries under restrictions or sanctions
  • Non-compliance can result in significant fines and legal penalties
exam tips

key takeaways

  • Due diligence = before; due care = ongoing. This distinction is directly testable. Diligence identifies risks ahead of time. Care maintains controls continuously after action is taken.
  • Wassenaar Arrangement = export controls for dual-use technologies. Strong encryption (VPNs, firewalls) qualifies as dual-use. Know the arrangement's name and its connection to cybersecurity tools.
  • Legal hold = preserve; e-discovery = search. A legal hold stops deletion. E-discovery is the subsequent process of finding relevant data within what was preserved.
  • Cross-jurisdictional compliance follows the data, not the headquarters. If your users or data are in a jurisdiction, that jurisdiction's laws may apply to you regardless of where your business is registered.
  • Contractual obligations must explicitly address cross-border compliance. Contracts should name who bears responsibility for meeting local laws in each region the contract covers.
quick decision rules
  • Research and investigation before taking action → Due diligence
  • Ongoing patching, monitoring, maintenance → Due care
  • Preserving data that might be evidence → Legal hold
  • Searching preserved data for court relevance → E-discovery
  • International transfer of encryption or security tools → Export controls (Wassenaar Arrangement)
