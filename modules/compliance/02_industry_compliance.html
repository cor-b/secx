Industry Compliance

Sector-specific laws and frameworks that govern how organizations protect sensitive data. Government, healthcare, financial, and utilities each have distinct regulatory drivers.

overview

Every regulated sector shares the same goal — protect sensitive data and maintain trust — but the nature of what they protect shapes entirely different compliance regimes. Patient records, financial transactions, classified contractor data, and power grid control systems each carry distinct risk profiles and attract distinct regulatory frameworks.

four regulated sectors

01 Government Federal system security and CUI protection. FISMA, NIST RMF, CMMC.
02 Healthcare Patient health data privacy and electronic records security. HIPAA, HITECH.
03 Financial Fraud prevention, data integrity, payment card security. GLBA, SOX, PCI DSS.
04 Utilities Critical infrastructure protection for power grids and water systems. NERC-CIP, FERC.

government compliance

Federal agencies and their contractors must protect sensitive government systems and data. Two frameworks govern federal agencies directly; a third governs defense contractors handling sensitive unclassified information.

FISMA
Federal Information Security Management Act Requires all federal agencies to develop, document, and implement security programs to protect their information systems. Sets the legal baseline for federal cybersecurity.
NIST RMF
Risk Management Framework The methodology that supports FISMA compliance. Agencies categorize their information systems by risk level, select appropriate security controls, implement them, assess effectiveness, and continuously monitor. A structured, repeatable process for managing federal system risk.
CMMC
Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification Required for defense contractors handling Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI). CUI is sensitive data that is not classified but still legally protected. Contractors who fail CMMC requirements can be barred from future government contracts.

CMMC levels

  1. Basic Cyber Hygiene Password protection, backups, logging. Entry-level practices every contractor must have.
  2. Intermediate Practices Access control, configuration management, incident response. Builds on level 1 fundamentals.
  3. NIST SP 800-171 Implementation Full implementation of NIST SP 800-171 controls for CUI protection, including advanced threat detection and response.
  4. Proactive Security Threat hunting and proactive risk management. Measuring and refining defenses against advanced threats.
  5. Advanced / Optimized Adaptive security controls, supply chain security, and cyber threat intelligence to combat the most sophisticated threat actors.

healthcare compliance

Healthcare organizations handle some of the most sensitive personal data. Regulations focus on privacy, access control, and the security of electronic records.

HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act Protects patient health information (PHI and ePHI). Sets strict rules on how hospitals, clinics, and insurers store, share, and access medical data. Violations carry heavy fines, civil or criminal charges, and reputational damage.
HITECH
Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health Act Builds on HIPAA by incentivizing adoption of electronic health records (EHR). Also strengthens HIPAA's security and privacy requirements and imposes tougher penalties on healthcare providers that fail to adequately protect patient data. Think of HITECH as HIPAA with stronger enforcement and a push toward digitization.

financial compliance

Financial regulations address three distinct risks: unauthorized access to personal financial data, fraudulent or inaccurate financial reporting, and payment card compromise.

GLBA
Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act Requires banks and financial institutions to protect customers' personal financial information. Also extends this obligation to third-party service providers: if a bank outsources a function, that vendor must also maintain adequate security measures.
SOX
Sarbanes-Oxley Act Applies to publicly traded companies. Requires accurate financial records and internal controls to prevent fraud in financial reporting. GLBA targets data privacy; SOX targets reporting integrity. Both are financial, but they regulate different risks.
PCI DSS
Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard Applies to any organization processing credit card transactions. A contractual requirement enforced by card networks, not a law. The six security goals: maintain a secure network, protect cardholder data through encryption, manage vulnerabilities, enforce access controls, monitor systems continuously, and maintain an information security policy.

utilities compliance

Utilities operate critical infrastructure where a cyber attack can have physical, public safety consequences. Regulations are strict and penalties are severe.

NERC-CIP
North American Electric Reliability Corporation Critical Infrastructure Protection Standards that secure the systems powering the electrical grid. Requires power companies to implement strong security practices preventing attackers from disrupting the grid. Non-compliance fines can reach $1 million per day per violation.
FERC
Federal Energy Regulatory Commission The oversight body that enforces compliance in the energy industry. Requires utility companies to conduct regular risk assessments and implement cybersecurity measures addressing both physical and cyber threats.

exam tips

key takeaways

  • FISMA applies to federal agencies. CMMC applies to defense contractors. Both are government, but different audiences. CMMC specifically protects CUI.
  • HIPAA is the base law; HITECH strengthens it and adds incentives for electronic health records. If the question involves EHR adoption or tougher HIPAA penalties, it's HITECH.
  • GLBA vs SOX: both financial, different risk. GLBA = customer data privacy. SOX = financial reporting integrity for public companies.
  • CMMC has 5 levels. Level 3 = full NIST SP 800-171 for CUI. Level 5 = adaptive, supply chain, threat intel.
  • NERC-CIP fines: up to $1 million per day per violation. The highest penalty figure in this section — if a question mentions $1M/day, it's NERC-CIP.
  • PCI DSS is contractual, not a law. Non-compliance means losing the ability to process card payments, not criminal liability.
  • FERC oversees, NERC-CIP sets the standards. FERC enforces compliance; NERC-CIP defines what compliance looks like for the grid.
quick decision rules
  • Federal agency security program → FISMA
  • Defense contractor handling CUI → CMMC
  • Patient health data privacy → HIPAA
  • Electronic health records + stronger penalties → HITECH
  • Bank customer financial data → GLBA
  • Public company financial reporting integrity → SOX
  • Credit card processing security → PCI DSS
  • Power grid cybersecurity, $1M/day fines → NERC-CIP
  • Energy sector oversight body → FERC
