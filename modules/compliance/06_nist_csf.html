NIST CSF

A flexible, industry-agnostic framework that organizes cybersecurity activities into five core functions: identify, protect, detect, respond, and recover.

overview

Developed by the National Institute of Standards and Technology, the NIST Cybersecurity Framework gives organizations a structured, repeatable approach to managing cyber risk regardless of industry or size. It does not prescribe specific tools, but it maps out what each function needs to accomplish and what types of capabilities support it.

The five functions are not linear steps — they operate continuously and in parallel. An organization is always identifying new assets, always protecting existing ones, always monitoring for threats.

the five core functions

  1. Identify Understand and manage risk to critical assets, systems, and data. Build an inventory of what exists before you can protect it.
  2. Protect Implement safeguards to limit the impact of a potential cyber event. Access controls, encryption, and user awareness training all live here.
  3. Detect Continuously monitor for suspicious activity and potential threats. Speed of detection directly affects impact of a breach.
  4. Respond Contain incidents, investigate root cause, notify stakeholders, and take action to limit damage.
  5. Recover Restore systems and data, apply lessons learned, and strengthen defenses to improve resilience against future incidents.

functions, goals, and tools

Each function maps to a security goal and is supported by a category of tools. The tools listed below are representative examples commonly cited in practice and on the exam.

FunctionGoalExample Tools
Identify Asset inventory and risk assessment SolarWinds, Lansweeper (asset mgmt); RSA Archer, RiskLens (risk assessment)
Protect Access control and data safeguards Cisco ASA, Palo Alto (firewalls); BitLocker, VeraCrypt (encryption); Okta (IAM)
Detect Continuous monitoring for threats Snort (IDS); Splunk (SIEM)
Respond Incident containment and investigation IBM Resilient, CrowdStrike (incident response platforms)
Recover Restoration and resilience improvement Veeam, Acronis (backup and recovery)

applied example: GreenTeaCare

A fictitious healthcare provider applying NIST CSF illustrates how the five functions work together end-to-end.

identify

GreenTeaCare uses asset management tools to map all systems that store patient data and risk assessment platforms like RSA Archer to identify where vulnerabilities exist across those systems.

protect

Firewalls restrict network access, patient records are encrypted at rest, and an IAM solution ensures only authorized clinical staff can access sensitive health information. Access is role-based and logged.

detect

The IT team monitors network traffic in real time using a SIEM. Alerts fire when access patterns deviate from baselines — for example, a login from an unrecognized device at 2 AM accessing bulk patient records.

respond

When an alert is confirmed as a breach, the incident response plan activates. CrowdStrike isolates affected systems, the IR team investigates root cause, and affected patients and regulators are notified per HIPAA breach notification rules.

recover

Backup tools restore any encrypted or deleted data. A post-incident review documents what happened, how detection could have been faster, and what controls to strengthen before returning to normal operations.

exam tips

key takeaways

  • Five functions in order: identify, protect, detect, respond, recover. The order matters for the exam — many questions test which function a described activity belongs to.
  • Identify = knowing what you have. Asset management and risk assessment are identify-function activities, not protect.
  • SIEM (Splunk) belongs to detect. A common distractor places monitoring tools in the protect function — they belong in detect.
  • CrowdStrike and IBM Resilient are respond tools, even though CrowdStrike also does endpoint protection. In the NIST CSF context, its incident response capability is the relevant function.
  • Recover includes lessons learned, not just restoring data. Improvement of future defenses is part of the recover function.
  • NIST CSF is flexible and non-prescriptive — it does not mandate specific tools or tell you exactly how to implement each function. It provides the structure; the organization chooses the implementation.
quick decision rules
  • Asset inventory, risk assessment → Identify
  • Firewalls, encryption, access controls, IAM → Protect
  • IDS, SIEM, continuous monitoring → Detect
  • Incident containment, root cause analysis, notifications → Respond
  • Backup restoration, post-incident review, resilience improvements → Recover
