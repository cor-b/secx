Industry Standards

Cross-industry guidelines developed by regulatory bodies and international organizations. PCI DSS secures payment card data, ISO 27000 governs information security management, and the Digital Markets Act regulates digital platform competition in the EU.

overview

Unlike sector-specific compliance laws, these standards cut across industries. PCI DSS applies to any organization that touches credit card transactions. The ISO 27000 series is a globally recognized benchmark for information security management that any organization can adopt. The Digital Markets Act targets the largest digital platforms operating in the EU, intersecting with data security obligations as part of its competition framework.

three standards

01 PCI DSS Six security goals for any org handling credit card transactions. Four merchant compliance levels based on transaction volume.
02 ISO 27000 Series ~60 international standards for information security management. Not legally binding, but globally recognized. ISO 27001 is the flagship.
03 Digital Markets Act EU regulation targeting "gatekeeper" platforms. Promotes fair competition and imposes data security obligations on large tech companies.

PCI DSS

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard applies to any business that handles credit card transactions, from a small online store to a global retailer. It is a contractual requirement enforced by card networks, not a law. Non-compliance results in losing the ability to process card payments, fines, and legal liability.

six security goals

  1. Build and maintain a secure network and systems
  2. Protect cardholder data through encryption
  3. Maintain a vulnerability management program
  4. Enforce strong access control measures
  5. Regularly monitor and test networks
  6. Maintain an information security policy

four merchant levels

Merchants are tiered by annual transaction volume. Higher volume means stricter validation requirements.

LevelTransactions / YearValidation Method
Level 1 Over 6 million Annual on-site audit by a Qualified Security Assessor (QSA) + annual compliance report
Level 2 1 million to 6 million Self-Assessment Questionnaire (SAQ); possible quarterly scans by Approved Scanning Vendor (ASV)
Level 3 20,000 to 1 million Self-Assessment Questionnaire (SAQ); possible quarterly scans by ASV
Level 4 Under 20,000 e-commerce or under 1 million overall Self-Assessment Questionnaire (SAQ); possible quarterly scans by ASV

ISO 27000 series

A family of approximately 60 international standards developed by ISO/IEC covering every layer of an organization's information security: networks, policies, controls, cloud, and risk management. Not legally required, but widely adopted as a global benchmark.

key standards in the series

ISO 27001
Information Security Management System (ISMS) The flagship standard. Defines the requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an ISMS. This is the standard organizations certify against — auditors verify ISO 27001 compliance, not the broader series.
ISO 27002
Security controls implementation guidance Provides best practices for implementing the security controls that ISO 27001 defines. Think of 27001 as the "what" and 27002 as the "how."
ISO 27005
Information security risk management Guidance on identifying, assessing, and treating information security risks. Used alongside 27001 to build the risk management component of an ISMS.
ISO 27017
Security controls for cloud services Extends 27002 with cloud-specific guidance for both cloud service providers and cloud service customers.
ISO 27018
Protection of personal data in cloud environments Focuses specifically on personally identifiable information (PII) processed by cloud service providers acting as data processors.

Digital Markets Act

The Digital Markets Act is an EU regulation targeting large technology companies, called gatekeepers, that control key digital services such as search engines, social networks, and app stores. Examples include Google, Apple, and Meta.

what it requires

  • Fair access to gatekeeper platforms and data for third-party businesses and developers
  • Interoperability: smaller messaging app providers must be able to connect with major platforms like WhatsApp
  • No self-preferencing: gatekeepers cannot rank their own products higher than competitors' in search results or app stores
  • High levels of data protection and security as part of fair competition obligations

enforcement

  • Enforced by the European Commission
  • Fines up to 10% of global annual revenue for non-compliance
  • Repeated violations can result in structural remedies, including breakup orders
  • Intersects with GDPR and PCI DSS by adding additional data security obligations for qualifying platforms
exam tips

key takeaways

  • PCI DSS Level 1 is the only level requiring an on-site audit by a QSA. Levels 2-4 use the SAQ. Know this distinction.
  • ISO 27001 = ISMS (the certifiable standard). ISO 27002 = how to implement the controls. ISO 27005 = risk management. ISO 27017 = cloud. ISO 27018 = cloud PII.
  • ISO 27000 is not legally binding for most organizations. Adoption is voluntary but widely expected by clients and regulators.
  • The DMA term to know is gatekeeper: a company controlling a key digital service. Google, Apple, Meta are the examples.
  • DMA fines: up to 10% of global revenue. Repeated violations can result in breakup orders.
  • DMA is EU-only but has extraterritorial reach: any gatekeeper serving EU users must comply regardless of where the company is headquartered.
quick decision rules
  • Merchant processing 6M+ card transactions → PCI DSS Level 1, QSA on-site audit
  • Merchant using Self-Assessment Questionnaire → PCI DSS Level 2, 3, or 4
  • Establishing an ISMS → ISO 27001
  • Implementing ISMS security controls → ISO 27002
  • Information security risk management guidance → ISO 27005
  • Cloud security controls → ISO 27017
  • Cloud PII/personal data protection → ISO 27018
  • EU regulation targeting large tech platforms → Digital Markets Act (DMA)
  • Company controlling a key digital service → Gatekeeper (DMA definition)
