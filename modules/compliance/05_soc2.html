SOC 2

An AICPA auditing framework that evaluates how well an organization protects customer data across five trust service criteria. Type I assesses design; Type II assesses ongoing effectiveness. SOC 3 is the public-facing summary.

overview

SOC 2 (Systems and Organization Controls 2) was developed by the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants to give technology and cloud service companies a standardized way to demonstrate that their data protection controls work. A SOC 2 report is what enterprise customers ask for when they want independent assurance that a vendor is trustworthy.

The framework offers two evaluation types that answer different questions: Type I asks "are the controls correctly designed?" and Type II asks "did they actually work over time?" SOC 3 packages the same assurance for public consumption.

three report types

01 SOC 2 Type I Point-in-time snapshot. Verifies that controls are properly designed, not whether they work consistently.
02 SOC 2 Type II 6-12 month evaluation. Verifies design AND operational effectiveness over time. The stronger, more trusted report.
03 SOC 3 Public-facing summary of a SOC 2. High-level overview without technical details. Shared freely with customers and partners.

trust service criteria

SOC 2 evaluations are structured around five categories called trust service criteria. Every SOC 2 report covers at least the security criterion; the others are included as relevant to the organization's services.

Security
System is protected against unauthorized access The mandatory criterion. Covers logical and physical access controls, network security, and threat monitoring. All SOC 2 reports must include this criterion.
Availability
System is reliable and accessible when needed Covers uptime commitments, performance monitoring, and incident response as it relates to service availability. Relevant for cloud providers and SaaS platforms.
Processing Integrity
Data is processed accurately, completely, and in a timely manner Ensures that data processing is authorized, complete, and free from errors or unauthorized manipulation. Important for financial transaction processors and data pipelines.
Confidentiality
Sensitive information is protected from unauthorized disclosure Covers data classification, access controls, and encryption practices for information designated as confidential under contract or policy.
Privacy
Personal data is collected, stored, and managed appropriately Addresses how personally identifiable information (PII) is handled throughout its lifecycle, including collection consent, retention, and disposal. Aligns with GDPR and CCPA requirements.

SOC 2 Type I

A Type I report evaluates the design of security controls at a single point in time. The auditor examines whether the right controls exist and are set up correctly — like verifying that encryption is configured and access controls are in place.

  • Does not evaluate whether controls functioned consistently or reliably over any period
  • A snapshot, not a longitudinal study
  • Faster and less expensive to obtain than Type II
  • Often used by organizations early in their compliance journey before they have enough operational history for Type II

SOC 2 Type II

A Type II report evaluates both the design and the operational effectiveness of controls over a period typically spanning 6 to 12 months. The auditor reviews whether controls functioned correctly and consistently throughout the entire evaluation window.

  • If a company claims to monitor for unauthorized access, Type II confirms those monitoring systems actually ran and caught events during the full period
  • Significantly more credible than Type I because it reflects sustained performance, not a single moment
  • The report that enterprise customers and large partners typically require before sharing sensitive data with a vendor
  • Requires operational maturity: the organization must have been running its controls long enough for a meaningful evaluation period

SOC 3

A SOC 3 report is a simplified, public-facing version of a SOC 2 report. It covers the same five trust service criteria but omits the technical control details, making it safe to share publicly without revealing security architecture.

  • Designed for customers, partners, and prospects who want assurance without needing the full technical report
  • Posted on company websites, included in vendor security questionnaires
  • Does not replace SOC 2 for enterprise due diligence — it supplements it
  • Think of it as the press release version of a SOC 2 audit

exam tips

key takeaways

  • Five trust service criteria: security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, privacy. Security is mandatory; the rest are optional but commonly included.
  • Type I = design, point in time. Type II = design + effectiveness, 6-12 months. This distinction is the most tested SOC 2 concept.
  • Type II is stronger because it proves controls worked consistently over time, not just that they existed on audit day.
  • SOC 3 is public. SOC 2 (either type) is restricted to customers and partners under NDA. If a question asks what a company posts publicly to demonstrate security, it's SOC 3.
  • SOC 2 was developed by the AICPA, not NIST. If the question mentions a CPA-administered audit framework, it's SOC 2.
quick decision rules
  • Controls exist and are designed correctly, checked once → SOC 2 Type I
  • Controls worked consistently for 6-12 months → SOC 2 Type II
  • Public-facing security assurance, no technical details → SOC 3
  • Customer asks for audit proof before signing a vendor contract → SOC 2 Type II (most likely)
  • Five criteria, one mandatory → SOC 2 trust service criteria; security is always required
