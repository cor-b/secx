Security Frameworks

Guidelines, best practices, and standards that help organizations manage cyber risk. Covers the landscape of foundational frameworks, the baseline-vs-benchmark distinction, and CIS Benchmarks as a practical hardening tool.

overview

Security frameworks give organizations a structured way to build and evaluate their defenses. Foundational best practices are the broad reference frameworks every security program draws on. Benchmarks measure how well your internal configuration baseline holds up against external standards. CIS Benchmarks translate those standards into actionable, system-specific hardening checklists.

three layers

01 Foundational Best Practices Core frameworks (ISO 27000, NIST CSF, COBIT, etc.) that define the principles and controls a security program should be built on.
02 Benchmarks and Baselines Internal baselines set the organization's minimum standard; external benchmarks provide the comparison point to verify those standards are sufficient.
03 CIS Benchmarks Prescriptive, system-specific hardening guides covering OSes, cloud platforms, and applications. The practical output of benchmark thinking.

foundational best practices

Foundational frameworks define what good security looks like at an organizational level. They are the reference layer — each one covers a different domain, and most mature security programs draw on several simultaneously.

baselines and benchmarks

These two terms are closely related but describe different things. Understanding the distinction is important for both the exam and practical security work.

Configuration Baseline
The minimum set of security configurations an organization puts in place This is your internal standard — the floor. It defines what "good enough" looks like within the organization. A baseline is set internally and reflects decisions about what controls are required before any system is considered secure enough to operate.
Benchmark
An external standard used to measure how well your baseline compares to industry best practices Once you have an internal baseline, a benchmark is the external reference that tells you whether your standards are actually rigorous enough. If your baseline says "require 8-character passwords" but the benchmark recommends 14 characters with complexity requirements, you know where to improve.

operational baselines

Beyond configuration, organizations also establish operational baselines that define what normal activity looks like at runtime — not just what's configured, but what's actually happening.

  • Example: network utilization averaging 80% during business hours is the baseline; a spike to 95% triggers investigation
  • A spike could indicate data exfiltration or simply that new employees were hired and bandwidth demand increased
  • If the cause is benign and persistent, the baseline should be updated to reflect the new normal

continuous monitoring

Operational baselines only have value if the organization is actively watching for deviations. Continuous monitoring is the practice of ongoing observation to detect abnormal events as they happen, not weeks or months later.

  • You cannot identify abnormal behavior without first knowing what normal looks like — that is why a solid operational baseline is a prerequisite
  • Example: users normally log in 8 AM to 5 PM; a new account logging in nightly between 1-3 AM is flagged as suspicious
  • Reviewing logs only once every six months means an attacker could operate undetected for months before discovery
  • Baselines are not permanent; update them when operational patterns legitimately change

CIS benchmarks

The Center for Internet Security publishes CIS Benchmarks: globally recognized, step-by-step hardening guides for specific systems, applications, and cloud environments. They are the practical implementation of the benchmark concept.

how they work

Each CIS Benchmark is tailored to a specific technology. If you are running Windows Server 2022, there is a dedicated benchmark that prescribes exactly which settings to configure: password policy requirements, which services to disable, how to tighten user access controls, and more. The benchmark acts as a checklist derived from widely tested security configurations.

  • Coverage includes operating systems (Windows, Linux, macOS), cloud environments (AWS, Azure, GCP), databases, web browsers, and network devices
  • Organized into two profile levels: Level 1 (basic, low-impact) and Level 2 (more restrictive, for high-security environments)
  • After hardening against a CIS Benchmark, an organization has documented, reportable evidence of compliance with that standard

relationship to baselines

After establishing an internal configuration baseline, use a CIS Benchmark to validate it. The benchmark shows whether the internal standard is aligned with global best practices, and where the gaps are.

exam tips

key takeaways

  • Baseline = internal minimum standard. Benchmark = external comparison point. The baseline is what you set; the benchmark is how you verify it.
  • Operational baseline defines normal runtime behavior (traffic, logins, usage patterns). Used to make continuous monitoring meaningful.
  • Continuous monitoring requires a baseline to work — without knowing normal, you cannot identify abnormal.
  • CIS Benchmarks are system-specific and prescriptive. They give step-by-step instructions, not just general guidance. If a question describes detailed hardening checklists for a specific OS or application, it is describing CIS Benchmarks.
  • Baselines can and should be updated when operational conditions legitimately change — they are not permanent.
  • COBIT is for IT governance. ITIL is for IT service management. These are often confused — COBIT aligns IT with business strategy, ITIL aligns IT services with business needs.
quick decision rules
  • Internal minimum security configuration → Configuration baseline
  • External standard to compare your baseline against → Benchmark
  • What normal network traffic looks like → Operational baseline
  • Step-by-step OS hardening checklist → CIS Benchmark
  • IT governance and business alignment → COBIT
  • IT service management → ITIL
