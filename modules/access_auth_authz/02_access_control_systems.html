Access Control Systems

Physical mechanisms that control entry to spaces, and logical mechanisms that control entry to networks — both verifying identity through something you know, have, or are.

overview

Access control systems regulate who can enter physical locations or connect to digital systems. Physical systems manage entry to spaces like buildings and server rooms using RFID cards, biometric scanners, and keypad entry, often combined in an access control vestibule. Logical systems manage digital access using passwords, multi-factor authentication, and smart cards. Both rely on the same three authentication factors: something you know, something you have, and something you are.

type 01

Physical

Controls entry to locations: buildings, server rooms, secure areas. Uses RFID, biometrics, keypads, and access control vestibules to verify identity before granting physical access.

type 02

Logical

Controls access to networks, systems, and data. Uses passwords, MFA, and smart cards to authenticate users before granting access to digital resources.

physical access control systems

Physical access control systems verify identity before granting entry to a physical location. They range from simple keypad-based systems to multi-factor configurations combining biometrics and RFID.

rfid key cards

RFID (Radio Frequency Identification) key cards use electromagnetic fields to identify the cardholder. When the card is held near a reader, the reader sends a radio signal that powers the chip inside the card. The chip transmits its unique ID back to the reader, which checks it against an access database. If authorized, entry is granted. Used in office buildings, hotels, and parking garages.

biometric scanners

Biometric systems verify identity using unique biological traits: fingerprints, facial recognition, iris scans, or retina scans. Because biometric traits cannot be loaned or easily forged, they provide stronger assurance than cards alone. Common in data centers, government facilities, and any environment requiring high confidence in who is entering.

keypad entry

Keypad systems require the user to enter a unique PIN. Often used in combination with RFID or biometrics as an additional verification layer rather than a standalone mechanism.

access control vestibule (mantrap)

An access control vestibule (also called a mantrap) consists of two sets of doors with a small enclosed space between them. The outer door must close and lock before the inner door can open, ensuring only one person passes through at a time. This prevents tailgating, where an unauthorized person follows an authorized person through a secured door without separate authentication.

  • Used in high-security environments: data centers, financial institutions, government buildings
  • Verification method (RFID, biometric, keypad, or combination) is required at each door
  • Additional verification may be required inside the vestibule while both doors are locked

logical access control systems

Logical access control systems regulate digital access to networks, systems, and data. Authentication methods correspond to the three factors: something you know, something you have, or something you are.

passwords

Passwords are the most common logical access mechanism. They represent "something you know." Limitations include reuse across platforms, weak construction, and phishing vulnerability. Organizations enforce complexity requirements and regular rotation to reduce these risks, though MFA is generally a stronger solution.

multi-factor authentication (MFA)

MFA requires two or more authentication factors from different categories. Combining factors dramatically reduces the risk of account compromise even when one factor (typically a password) is breached.

something you know
Password, PIN, or security question answer. The weakest factor in isolation. Can be phished, guessed, or stolen without physical access to the user.
something you have
Smartphone (OTP via authenticator app or SMS), hardware security token, smart card. Requires the attacker to physically possess the device, or intercept the token in real time.
something you are
Biometric trait: fingerprint, facial recognition, iris scan, voice recognition. Tied to the individual's body. Cannot be transferred, but can be spoofed by sophisticated attackers or fail due to environmental factors.

smart cards

A smart card is a physical card with an embedded chip containing encrypted user identity data. The user inserts or taps the card on a reader, which reads the chip to authenticate. Smart cards typically work alongside a PIN (something you have + something you know), providing two-factor authentication. Used in corporate environments and government offices to secure computer login, network access, and physical entry.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Physical access control = entry to physical locations. Logical access control = access to digital systems and data.
  • RFID: reader sends radio signal, powers chip in card, chip returns unique ID, checked against database.
  • Access control vestibule / mantrap: two-door design, one person at a time, prevents tailgating. Know both names.
  • Tailgating = unauthorized person follows authorized person through a door. The mantrap is the physical countermeasure.
  • Three MFA factors: something you know (password/PIN), something you have (token/phone/smart card), something you are (biometric). MFA requires at least two from different categories.
  • Smart card alone = something you have. Smart card + PIN = two-factor (have + know).
decision rules
  • Scenario involves preventing unauthorized physical entry to a secure area → access control vestibule / mantrap
  • Scenario involves preventing someone from following an authorized person through a door → tailgating prevention via mantrap
  • Scenario involves verifying identity via biological traits → biometric scanner
  • Scenario involves using two or more verification methods from different factor categories → MFA
  • Scenario involves a physical card with an embedded chip that also requires a PIN → smart card (two-factor)
  • Question asks which factor a fingerprint scanner represents → something you are
  • Question asks which factor an authenticator app represents → something you have
