Risk Response

Implementing controls to address identified risks. A three-step sequence: confirm the risk is real, measure how bad it could be, then act to reduce or eliminate it.

overview

Risk response turns assessment findings into action. Before resources are committed, a risk must be validated as real. Once confirmed, its severity impact determines urgency and prioritization. Then remediation applies the controls that reduce or eliminate the threat.

The three steps are sequential: you don't remediate what you haven't validated, and you don't allocate resources without understanding severity.

the three steps

01 Validation Confirm whether the identified risk is real or a false alarm before committing resources.
02 Severity Impact Quantify how much disruption a confirmed risk could cause to guide prioritization and urgency.
03 Remediation Take actionable steps to fix, reduce, or eliminate the confirmed, assessed risk.

risk validation

Validation answers one question: is this risk actually real? Alerts, reports, and threat intelligence all generate noise. Jumping to action on a false alarm wastes resources and can cause unnecessary disruption.

what validation looks like

An IT team receives an alert about a suspicious login. Before escalating, they investigate:

  • Check system logs for the source IP and location
  • Cross-reference with known employee travel or VPN use
  • Run diagnostics to look for indicators of compromise
  • Determine whether the login was a remote employee or an actual intruder
True Positive
The risk is real and requires a response Investigation confirms the alert reflects an actual threat. Proceed to severity impact assessment and remediation.
False Positive
The alert fired but no real threat exists Example: suspicious login turns out to be an employee connecting from an airport. No remediation needed, but the alert rule may need tuning to reduce future noise.

Validation ensures that remediation resources are spent on real problems, not phantom alerts. An unvalidated response can be as damaging as no response at all.

severity impact

Once a risk is confirmed as real, severity impact assessment determines how bad it could get. This drives how urgently and how heavily the organization needs to respond.

assessing impact

The same event can have very different severity depending on scale. A supplier delay of one day is a minor adjustment; a delay of one week can cascade into missed deadlines and lost revenue. The team asks:

  • What operations are affected if this risk is realized?
  • How many users, customers, or systems are exposed?
  • What is the financial, reputational, or regulatory consequence?
  • How quickly could things escalate if left unaddressed?

severity guides prioritization

High severity = urgent response, more resources. Low severity = scheduled remediation, lower resource allocation. Without a severity assessment, all risks look equally urgent and resource allocation breaks down.

  • High severity: phishing compromise of executive email, potential data exfiltration
  • Medium severity: single department affected, no sensitive data involved
  • Low severity: minor misconfiguration with no current exploitability

remediation

Remediation is the action phase: taking concrete steps to reduce or eliminate a confirmed, assessed risk. The response is scaled to the severity determined in the previous step.

example: phishing compromise

A company confirms that a phishing attack has compromised employee email accounts. Severity is assessed as high because sensitive data may be at risk. Remediation steps:

  1. Block the compromised accounts immediately to stop the bleeding
  2. Force password resets on all affected accounts
  3. Enable or enforce multifactor authentication across the affected group
  4. Conduct forensic review of email logs to identify what data was accessed
  5. Run employee security awareness training to reduce future susceptibility
  6. Harden email filtering rules to catch similar phishing patterns going forward

remediation outcomes

Mitigation
Controls reduce the risk to an acceptable level but do not eliminate it entirely Most common outcome. Residual risk remains and must be monitored. MFA on compromised accounts mitigates further access but doesn't undo what was already accessed.
Elimination
The risk is fully removed Rare in practice. Achieved by removing the vulnerable system, discontinuing the risky process, or patching a vulnerability with a complete fix. No residual risk remains.
Transfer
The financial impact of the risk is shifted to a third party Typically through cyber insurance. The risk event may still occur, but the organization's financial exposure is covered. Does not reduce likelihood or technical impact.
exam tips

key takeaways

  • Validation comes first, always. You don't assess severity or remediate a risk you haven't confirmed is real.
  • False positives are confirmed by validation. Wasting remediation effort on false positives is exactly what validation prevents.
  • Severity impact drives prioritization: the same category of risk can require immediate escalation or a scheduled fix depending on assessed impact.
  • Remediation does not always mean elimination. Most remediations result in mitigation with residual risk remaining.
  • The three steps in order: validate the risk is real, assess severity to set priority, remediate to reduce or remove it.
  • Employee training after an incident is a valid remediation step, it reduces future exposure even if it doesn't fix the current compromise.
quick decision rules
  • Alert fired, is it real? → Risk validation
  • Alert confirmed real, how bad? → Severity impact assessment
  • Confirmed + assessed, what do we do? → Remediation
  • Alert turns out to be an employee on VPN → False positive, no remediation needed
  • Risk reduced but not eliminated after controls → Mitigation (residual risk remains)
  • Financial exposure shifted via insurance → Risk transfer, not elimination
