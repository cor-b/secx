Risk Assessment Frameworks

Five frameworks for identifying, assessing, and managing enterprise risk. Each fits a different environment, so knowing the environment usually tells you the framework.

overview

Risk assessment frameworks are toolkits for managing risk. Five dominate the SecurityX exam: NIST RMF for federal and regulated sectors, ISO 27005 for international information security, COSO ERM for enterprise-wide strategic risk, OCTAVE for hands-on IT risk, and FAIR for quantifying risk in dollars. The simplest way to keep them straight: each has a distinctive environment or use case.

the five frameworks at a glance

  1. NIST RMF National Institute of Standards and Technology Risk Management Framework. Federal agencies, defense, healthcare. FISMA-aligned, continuous-monitoring driven. The "government-issued safety manual."
  2. ISO 27005 International standard for information security risk management. Companion to ISO 27001 (the ISMS standard). The "international rule book."
  3. COSO ERM Committee of Sponsoring Organizations Enterprise Risk Management. Ties risk management to business strategy across all domains. The "strategic planner for risk."
  4. OCTAVE Operationally Critical Threat, Asset, and Vulnerability Evaluation. Hands-on, internal IT infrastructure risk assessment. The "tactical operations guide."
  5. FAIR Factor Analysis of Information Risk. Quantitative; translates cyber risk into financial terms for executive and board communication. The "risk analyst toolset."

NIST RMF

NIST Risk Management Framework. A detailed step-by-step process for managing cybersecurity risks while staying aligned with U.S. federal standards.

Where it fits

  • Critical sectors: defense, healthcare, federal agencies
  • Organizations subject to FISMA (Federal Information Security Management Act)
  • Anywhere a structured, audit-ready risk process is required

Key characteristics

  • Step-by-step process for selecting, implementing, and monitoring security controls
  • Heavy emphasis on continuous monitoring and ongoing risk assessment
  • Controls are tailored to the organization's specific needs
  • Maps cleanly to NIST 800-53 control catalog and NIST CSF
memory hook

NIST RMF is the government-issued safety manual: detailed, prescriptive, focused on selecting and continuously monitoring controls. If the question mentions federal agencies, FISMA, or defense contractors, it's NIST RMF.

ISO 27005

International standard for information security risk management. A structured, vendor-neutral framework for identifying, assessing, and mitigating security risks throughout the information lifecycle.

Where it fits

  • Organizations that need an internationally recognized risk framework
  • Companies already pursuing or holding ISO 27001 certification (the ISMS standard)
  • Multinational orgs where U.S.-specific frameworks like NIST RMF don't apply

Key characteristics

  • Emphasizes documenting and evaluating risks systematically
  • Designed to complement ISO 27001 (which defines the Information Security Management System)
  • Information lifecycle perspective: identify → assess → treat → monitor
  • Pairs with ISO 27002 (security controls) for a complete information security stack
memory hook

ISO 27005 is the international rule book for information security risk. If the question mentions ISO 27001, ISMS, or an international standard, ISO 27005 is the risk-side answer.

COSO ERM

Enterprise Risk Management. Unlike the others, COSO ERM is not specific to cybersecurity. It covers risk across the entire organization: financial, operational, compliance, strategic, and yes, cyber.

Where it fits

  • Organizations integrating risk into corporate strategy, not just IT or security
  • Boards and senior leadership needing a unified view of enterprise-wide risk
  • Public companies (COSO is also the source of the internal control framework underpinning SOX compliance)

Key characteristics

  • Aligns risk management with business strategy and objectives
  • Strong emphasis on governance and decision-making at all levels
  • Risk is considered in both strategic planning and daily operations
  • Cross-domain: financial, operational, cybersecurity, compliance, reputational
memory hook

COSO ERM is the strategic planner for risk: broad in scope, tied to business strategy, used by boards and executives. If the question mentions enterprise-wide risk, governance, or business strategy alignment, it's COSO ERM.

OCTAVE

Operationally Critical Threat, Asset, and Vulnerability Evaluation. A hands-on, internal-perspective framework focused on the operational reality of an organization's IT infrastructure.

Where it fits

  • Organizations doing self-directed risk assessments (no external auditor required)
  • Risk assessment of specific IT assets rather than enterprise-wide strategy
  • Smaller and mid-sized orgs that need a practical, ground-level approach

Key characteristics

  • Internal perspective: the organization assesses itself, no external auditor required
  • Focus on assets, threats, and vulnerabilities specific to the operational environment
  • Produces a tailored risk management plan reflecting the org's actual operational needs
  • Three variants exist: OCTAVE, OCTAVE-S (small orgs), and OCTAVE Allegro (streamlined)
memory hook

OCTAVE is the tactical operations guide: hands-on, internal, asset-focused. If the question mentions internal risk assessment, IT infrastructure, or operational risk, it's OCTAVE.

FAIR

Factor Analysis of Information Risk. The quantitative framework. Translates cyber risk into financial terms so decision-makers can compare it to other business risks.

Where it fits

  • Communicating risk to non-technical stakeholders (executives, board members)
  • Justifying security investments in budget-allocation conversations
  • Organizations that want to compare cyber risk against financial risk on the same axis

Key characteristics

  • Risk is expressed in monetary terms: expected loss, loss frequency, loss magnitude
  • Decomposes risk into measurable factors (asset value, threat capability, control strength, loss event frequency)
  • Output is comparable to financial risk metrics, making cross-domain prioritization possible
  • Often used alongside other frameworks rather than instead of them
memory hook

FAIR is the risk analyst toolset: quantitative, financial, executive-facing. If the question mentions dollar amounts, ALE, financial impact, or board communication, it's FAIR.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • NIST RMF = federal / defense / healthcare. FISMA. Continuous monitoring.
  • ISO 27005 = international, information security risk; pairs with ISO 27001 (ISMS).
  • COSO ERM = enterprise-wide; ties risk to business strategy. Not cyber-specific.
  • OCTAVE = operational, internal, hands-on, asset-focused IT risk.
  • FAIR = quantitative, expresses risk in dollars, for board / executive communication.
  • The exam typically gives you a scenario or environment and asks which framework fits. Match the environment to the framework: federal → NIST RMF, ISO 27001 shop → ISO 27005, board-level risk → COSO ERM, internal IT → OCTAVE, dollars → FAIR.
  • Multiple frameworks can coexist: a company might use COSO ERM at the board level, NIST RMF for federal contracts, and FAIR when communicating to leadership.
quick decision rules
  • Federal agency, defense contractor, FISMA → NIST RMF
  • Continuous monitoring emphasis → NIST RMF
  • International standard for info security risk → ISO 27005
  • Companion / complement to ISO 27001 ISMS → ISO 27005
  • Enterprise-wide risk: financial + operational + cyber → COSO ERM
  • Align risk with corporate strategy / board view → COSO ERM
  • Internal, asset-focused IT risk assessment → OCTAVE
  • Self-directed, no external auditor → OCTAVE
  • Quantify risk in dollars / financial terms → FAIR
  • Explain cyber risk to non-technical executives → FAIR
