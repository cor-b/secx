Confidentiality Risk Considerations

Identifying and mitigating threats that could lead to unauthorized access or disclosure of sensitive information.

overview

Confidentiality is breached when unauthorized parties access sensitive or privileged data. Six considerations work together to reduce this risk: regular incident response testing, encryption at rest and in transit, formal handling of sensitive and privileged data breaches, a data leak response plan, and timely stakeholder reporting.

the six considerations

01 Incident Response Testing Simulate breaches to ensure the team can contain and recover quickly.
02 Encryption Make data unreadable without the key. Protects in transit and at rest.
03 Sensitive Data Breach Unauthorized access to PII like contact info or driver's license details.
04 Privileged Data Breach Unauthorized access to highly sensitive data like financial or health records.
05 Data Leak Response Formal plan to contain, fix, and notify after a confidentiality breach.
06 Reporting Communicate with internal teams and external stakeholders, including regulators.

Scenario: Riverbend Health

running example

Riverbend Health is a healthcare provider managing sensitive patient information including medical records and billing data. Their security team detected suspicious network activity indicating a potential breach.

Thanks to regular incident response testing, the team swiftly executed its prepared plan to contain the breach and investigate the source. During investigation, they discovered attackers had exploited a privileged account.

Riverbend had encrypted patient data, which should have made any captured information unreadable. But because the compromised privileged account gave attackers access to the decryption keys, the encryption was bypassed. The breach escalated to both a sensitive data breach (patient contact information) and a privileged data breach (medical histories and insurance details).

Riverbend activated its data leak response plan: notifying affected patients, applying security patches, and assessing how the breach occurred. Comprehensive reporting kept executives, regulatory bodies, and affected patients informed throughout.

Incident Response Testing

Simulating security breaches to validate and improve the organization's ability to detect, respond to, and recover from incidents. Treats the IR plan as a living document, not a binder on a shelf.

What it produces

  • Identifies weaknesses in response procedures before a real incident exposes them
  • Builds team muscle memory for containment and mitigation
  • Surfaces security gaps that can be closed proactively
  • Reduces the time between detection and effective response

Riverbend application: Because the team had tested their IR plan, they could execute it immediately when suspicious activity was detected. No improvisation under pressure.

Encryption

Cryptographic transformation that makes data unreadable without the correct decryption key. Essential for confidentiality, but only as strong as the protection around the key itself.

Where it applies

  • In transit: data moving across networks (TLS, IPsec, encrypted VPN tunnels)
  • At rest: data stored on disk, in databases, on backup media (AES-256, full-disk encryption, transparent database encryption)

How it fails

  • An attacker compromises a privileged account with access to keys
  • Decryption keys stored alongside the encrypted data (defeats the purpose)
  • Weak key management practices: keys never rotated, shared informally, written down
  • Cryptographic algorithms that have been broken or deprecated

Riverbend application: Encryption could have neutralized the breach, but the attackers gained access to the decryption keys through a compromised privileged account. Encryption without key protection is theatre.

Sensitive Data Breach

Unauthorized access to Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and similarly sensitive data: names, contact information, driver's license details, date of birth, addresses.

Why it matters

  • Exposed PII enables identity theft and fraud
  • Triggers breach notification laws in most jurisdictions
  • Damages reputation and customer trust

Mitigations

  • Strong access controls (role-based, least privilege)
  • Encryption at rest for stored PII
  • Prompt incident response to contain the breach
  • Data minimization: don't collect PII you don't need

Riverbend application: Patient contact information was exposed in the breach, qualifying as a sensitive data breach.

Privileged Data Breach

Unauthorized access to highly sensitive data: financial records, health information, classified data, intellectual property. Higher impact than a standard sensitive data breach because the data carries more risk per record.

Why it's more severe

  • Health records (HIPAA), financial records (SOX, GLBA), and similar categories trigger heavier regulatory scrutiny
  • Larger settlements and fines per incident
  • Harder for victims to recover (health histories can't be re-issued like a credit card)

Mitigations specific to privileged data

  • Multi-factor authentication on all privileged accounts
  • Data loss prevention (DLP) tools to detect and block exfiltration
  • Privileged account audits: who has access, when did they last use it, do they still need it
  • Privileged Access Management (PAM) for just-in-time elevation

Riverbend application: The sensitive data breach would have been less severe on its own (encrypted data wasn't directly readable), but the attackers' privileged account access escalated it to a privileged data breach by exposing medical histories and insurance details.

Data Leak Response

A formal plan to contain and mitigate the impact of a confidentiality breach. Reactive: this is what executes when something has actually leaked.

Core activities

  1. Identify the source of the leak
  2. Contain the exposure (isolate systems, revoke credentials, block traffic)
  3. Apply corrective measures to prevent further loss
  4. Preserve evidence for investigation and potential legal action
  5. Notify affected parties per legal and contractual obligations

Why a written plan matters

  • Removes ad-hoc decision-making from high-pressure moments
  • Coordinates response across IT, security, legal, PR, and exec teams
  • Minimizes the window where data continues to leak
  • Preserves stakeholder trust by demonstrating preparedness

Riverbend application: Their leak response plan was already in place. They didn't waste time figuring out who to call or what to do first.

Reporting

Communicating breach details to internal and external stakeholders. Timely, accurate, complete. Not the same as data leak response (which is operational containment) — reporting is the communication arm.

Who needs to be informed

  • Internal: management, executive leadership, legal counsel, board (for material breaches)
  • External: regulatory bodies (HHS for HIPAA, state AGs, SEC for public companies), affected individuals, business partners with right-to-know clauses, sometimes law enforcement

What good reporting enables

  • Quick, informed decision-making at every level
  • Compliance with legal obligations (most breach notification laws have strict timelines, often 72 hours under GDPR)
  • Coordinated response across teams that may not otherwise communicate
  • Transparent communication that preserves stakeholder trust
  • Root cause analysis to prevent recurrence

What inadequate reporting causes

  • Prolonged exposure of sensitive data (because no one outside the immediate team knows to act)
  • Regulatory penalties for late or missing notifications
  • Greater reputational damage when the breach eventually surfaces
  • Loss of stakeholder trust that's hard to rebuild

Riverbend application: Quick reporting let affected patients take protective actions and helped Riverbend meet regulatory obligations. Compare to the Uber 2016 case from lesson 12 of the governance section, where concealment cost $148M.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • Sensitive vs Privileged breach: Sensitive = PII (names, contact info, driver's license). Privileged = highly sensitive (financial, health, classified). Both are confidentiality breaches; privileged is the more severe.
  • Encryption is only as strong as key protection. If attackers compromise privileged accounts with key access, encryption is bypassed.
  • Incident Response Testing is preventive (validates the plan). Data Leak Response is reactive (executes the plan when a breach happens). Don't confuse them.
  • Reporting is both internal AND external. Internal = leadership, legal, board. External = regulators, affected individuals, partners.
  • Strong key management matters as much as encryption itself. Store keys separately from data; rotate regularly; tightly control who can access them.
  • Privileged data breaches trigger heavier regulatory scrutiny: HIPAA for health, SOX/GLBA for financial.
quick decision rules
  • PII exposed (contact info, ID numbers) → Sensitive data breach
  • Financial or health records exposed → Privileged data breach
  • Validate the IR plan before something goes wrong → Incident response testing
  • Act on an active breach right now → Data leak response
  • Inform regulators and affected individuals → Reporting
  • Encryption failed because attackers got the keys → Privileged account compromise; tighten access controls and key management
  • "72 hours" notification deadline → GDPR breach reporting
