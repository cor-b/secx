Availability Risk Considerations

Ensuring critical systems and data remain accessible during and after disruptive events. Plan it, back it up two different ways, and test it.

overview

Availability depends on four pieces working together: BC/DR planning to identify what must keep running, connected backups for fast failover, disconnected backups for ransomware resistance, and regular testing to verify it all actually works under pressure.

the four considerations

01 BC/DR Planning Identify mission-essential functions and the critical assets that support them. Map disaster scenarios to recovery strategies.
02 Connected Backups Network-attached mirrors. Synchronous for hot sites, asynchronous for warm sites.
03 Disconnected Backups Off-network copies that survive ransomware. The 3-2-1 rule lives here.
04 BC/DR Testing Simulate disruptions to validate the plan and surface gaps before they matter.

Scenario: EverTrust Financial

running example

EverTrust Financial is a global financial services company processing billions of dollars in daily transactions. Downtime costs them revenue, regulatory exposure, and customer trust.

Their BC/DR plans include alternate operation sites, recovery workflows for critical services, and data restoration procedures for natural disasters or system failures.

Day to day, they use connected backups that mirror real-time transaction data to multiple data centers. If one location goes down, transactions continue at another.

But because connected backups can be reached by ransomware, EverTrust also keeps disconnected backups offline. If production systems are compromised, the offline copies restore clean, unaltered data.

Finally, they run regular BC/DR testing through real-world simulations. Gaps in the plan get found in drills, not in disasters.

BC/DR Planning

Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery planning starts with understanding what would happen if the business couldn't perform its core work, then builds the strategies that prevent that situation.

The planning chain

  1. Business Impact Analysis (BIA) A process that identifies and evaluates the effects of an organization not being able to maintain its Mission-Essential Functions. The BIA is the starting point of all BC/DR planning.
  2. Mission-Essential Functions (MEFs) The business's core activities, necessary to fulfill its strategic mission. For EverTrust, processing transactions is an MEF. For a hospital, providing patient care.
  3. Critical assets The systems, data, and processes that each MEF relies on. These must remain operational (or be quickly restored) during a disruption.
  4. Recovery strategies Tactical plans to protect and maintain critical assets. Backup sites, redundant systems, failover procedures, alternate communication channels.

Disasters typically planned for

  • Natural disasters: hurricanes, earthquakes, floods, wildfires
  • Cyberattacks: ransomware, DDoS, destructive malware
  • Power outages: grid failures, regional blackouts
  • Pandemics: workforce unavailability, remote work scaling

Backup site types

A key planning output is identifying backup sites where systems can be restored if the primary location goes down. Four types, ordered by readiness and cost:

Hot Site
Fully operational real-time backup location. Critical systems and production data are continuously mirrored. Immediate failover if the primary site fails. Most expensive option. Used where there is zero tolerance for downtime, e.g. financial trading floors or military operations supporting national infrastructure.
Warm Site
Technical infrastructure in place, but requires data restoration to become fully operational. A warm site and a hot site might look identical physically. The difference: a warm site doesn't have an up-to-date copy of production data. Cheaper than hot, faster than cold. Recovery in hours to a day.
Cold Site
Bare facility, may offer only basic infrastructure like power and cooling. A designated location to rebuild. May be nothing more than a lease for space where the organization can stand up new equipment after a disaster. Cheapest, but longest recovery time, often days to weeks.
Cloud Site
Cloud-based services that host data and applications. Flexibility and scalability are the draw. Cloud sites often combine elements of hot and warm sites because cloud infrastructure provides high availability and on-demand provisioning. Increasingly the default choice for new BC/DR plans.

Connected Backups

Backups that are continuously attached to the production network. Fast for recovery, vulnerable to attacks that target the production environment.

Synchronous

Synchronous backup
Copies data in real time. Every change on the primary system is instantly mirrored to the backup. Zero data loss in a failover (or near-zero). Ideal for supporting hot sites. Requires high-speed, low-latency network links between primary and backup, which makes synchronous backup the most expensive option.

Asynchronous

Asynchronous backup
Transmits backup data with a slight delay, allowing changes to accumulate before being sent. Some data loss is possible (whatever changed since the last sync). Useful for supporting warm sites where real-time updates aren't required but timely recovery still matters. Cheaper bandwidth requirements than synchronous.

Disconnected Backups

Backups that are not continuously connected to the production network. Sometimes called "air-gapped" or "offline" backups. Their isolation is the whole point: an attack that reaches the production network can't reach them.

Why disconnected matters

  • Ransomware resistance: modern ransomware actively hunts and encrypts connected backups. Offline copies are out of reach.
  • Insider threat resistance: even a malicious admin with full production access can't tamper with a backup that's physically disconnected.
  • Confidence in clean restore: when production is compromised, you need a known-good source. Disconnected backups provide that.
the 3-2-1 rule

A widely cited backup strategy that includes a disconnected component:

3 copies of the data, on 2 different storage media types, with 1 copy stored off-site.

The off-site copy is typically the disconnected backup. Even if local systems fail or are compromised, a clean and untouched copy of the data remains accessible. Common exam answer.

BC/DR Testing

Recovery plans that haven't been tested don't work. Testing validates that the strategies on paper can actually be executed under real conditions.

What testing covers

  • Shifting operations to a backup data center or alternate office
  • Recovering critical systems within target time objectives
  • Restoring data from backups (connected and disconnected)
  • Communications during disruption (when normal channels are down)
  • Employee role clarity: who does what during a crisis

What testing surfaces

  • Communication breakdowns when the usual systems are unavailable
  • Equipment issues at backup sites (hardware that didn't actually work)
  • Resource shortages: not enough staff, capacity, or vendor support
  • Plan drift: documented procedures that no longer match the current environment
  • Skill gaps: people who don't know what their role requires

Why it's mandatory, not optional

If a plan is never tested, the first time it runs in earnest is during a real disaster. That is the worst possible moment to discover that the backup site's network drops cost more bandwidth than provisioned or that nobody knows the door code to the cold site.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • The planning chain: BIA → MEFs → critical assets → recovery strategies. Each step depends on the one before it.
  • Hot site = real-time and ready. Warm = infrastructure ready, no data. Cold = bare facility. Cloud = flexible, usually hot/warm equivalent.
  • Synchronous backup pairs with hot sites (real-time, expensive). Asynchronous pairs with warm sites (delayed, cheaper).
  • Connected backups are vulnerable to ransomware; disconnected backups are the safety net.
  • 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media types, 1 off-site. Memorize the numbers.
  • A BIA evaluates the impact of not being able to maintain MEFs. It's the starting point of BC/DR planning.
  • If you don't test the plan, it doesn't work. Testing is mandatory, not optional.
quick decision rules
  • Zero downtime tolerance, real-time failover → Hot site (with synchronous backup)
  • Infrastructure ready but data must be restored → Warm site (with asynchronous backup)
  • Bare facility, longest recovery → Cold site
  • Flexible, scalable, often cloud-native → Cloud site
  • Survive a ransomware attack → Disconnected backup
  • 3 copies / 2 media / 1 off-site → 3-2-1 rule
  • "Effects of not being able to maintain MEFs" → Business Impact Analysis (BIA)
  • "Core activities necessary to fulfill the mission" → Mission-Essential Functions (MEFs)
  • Validate that the plan actually works → BC/DR Testing
