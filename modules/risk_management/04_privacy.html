Privacy Risk Considerations

Managing the potential for unauthorized access, disclosure, or misuse of personal and sensitive information. Three angles: biometrics, the legal rights of data subjects, and the jurisdictional reach of data law.

overview

Privacy risk is the potential for unauthorized access, disclosure, or misuse of personal data. Three considerations cover the surface area: the unique risks of biometric data (permanent, can't be reissued), the legal rights of data subjects under modern privacy laws, and data sovereignty, the principle that data follows the laws of the country where it's collected or stored.

the three considerations

01 Biometrics Fingerprint, face, iris, voice. Sensitive and permanent. Tuned via FAR, FRR, and the Crossover Error Rate.
02 Data Subject Rights Legal protections that let individuals access, correct, delete, and transfer their data. GDPR, CCPA, SHIELD.
03 Data Sovereignty Data is subject to the laws of the country where it's collected or stored. Cross-border data creates compliance overlap.

Scenario: BioStar 2 (2019)

case study

The BioStar 2 data breach exposed over 27.8 million biometric records including fingerprints and facial recognition data. Because biometric data is a permanent identifier (you can't reissue your face), exposure poses serious long-term risks.

Under GDPR, biometric data is classified as a "special category of data" requiring stronger safeguards. The breach raised questions about whether Suprema (the company behind BioStar 2) had complied with GDPR.

One of Suprema's clients was the UK's Metropolitan Police, and at the time of the breach the UK was still part of the EU. Because data was exposed across multiple countries, the privacy laws of several jurisdictions were simultaneously in effect, a textbook data sovereignty problem.

The case touches all three considerations in this lesson: biometric data (permanent and sensitive), data subject rights (millions of affected individuals across jurisdictions), and data sovereignty (overlapping legal frameworks).

Biometrics

Biometric verification uses unique physical or behavioral traits as identity proof. Common modalities:

  • Fingerprint scans
  • Facial recognition
  • Iris scans
  • Voice recognition

Biometrics play an important role in identity verification but introduce privacy risks because the data is highly sensitive and permanent. Compromised passwords can be changed; compromised fingerprints cannot.

The two key metrics

FAR: False Acceptance Rate
How often unauthorized individuals are mistakenly identified as legitimate users. High FAR = security problem. Unauthorized users slip through. This is the metric that matters most in adversarial contexts.
FRR: False Rejection Rate
How often legitimate users are wrongly denied access. High FRR = usability problem. Authorized users get locked out, frustrated, and start looking for workarounds (which themselves create security holes).

Sensitivity adjustment

FAR and FRR move in opposite directions as sensitivity changes. Tightening the system to catch unauthorized users also rejects more legitimate ones:

SensitivityFRR (rejections)FAR (false accepts)Effect
Higher (stricter)↑ rises↓ fallsMore secure, less usable
Lower (lenient)↓ falls↑ risesMore usable, less secure

Crossover Error Rate (CER)

the sweet spot

The Crossover Error Rate is the point where the FAR and FRR are equal. On a graph of sensitivity vs error rate, it's where the two curves intersect.

Tuning sensitivity near the CER minimizes both errors simultaneously, balancing security and user convenience. A lower CER means a better biometric system: fewer total errors at the optimal sensitivity.

Data Subject Rights

Data subject rights are legal protections that give individuals control over their personal information. The "data subject" is the person whose data is being processed. Three notable laws define these rights:

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)
EU law applying to EU residents' data, anywhere in the world. Subjects can access, correct, delete, and transfer their personal data (the "right to be forgotten" and data portability). Organizations must inform subjects how their data will be used before collection. Biometric data is classified as a "special category" requiring stronger safeguards.
CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act)
California state law applying to California residents. Subjects have the right to know what data is being collected, request deletion of their data, and opt out of any sale of their data. Narrower than GDPR but covers similar core ground.
SHIELD Act (Stop Hacks and Improve Electronic Data Security)
New York state law focused on data security. Requires organizations to take reasonable steps to protect personal data and notify individuals in the event of a breach. Less about subject rights to access/delete and more about the organization's protective duties.

Data Sovereignty

Data sovereignty is the principle that data is subject to the laws and regulations of the country in which it is collected and/or stored. Data doesn't escape its jurisdiction just by moving servers.

Why it gets complicated

  • Organizations must comply with the laws of both the country where data originates and where it is stored or processed
  • For multinational companies, this means tracking multiple legal frameworks per dataset
  • GDPR is the most famous example: personal data of EU residents must comply with GDPR even if stored outside the EU

Worked example

An American company collects personal data from European customers and stores it on US-based servers. Because the data subjects are EU residents, GDPR still applies. The American company is bound by:

  • US laws governing data stored on US servers
  • GDPR, because the data originated from EU residents
  • Any state-level privacy laws (e.g. CCPA if California residents are involved)

Cross-border data adds compliance overhead and the possibility of conflicting requirements. Data sovereignty is why "where is your data" is now a board-level question, not just an IT one.

exam tips

Key Takeaways

  • FAR = unauthorized users accepted (security problem). FRR = legitimate users denied (usability problem). Mix these up at your peril.
  • CER (Crossover Error Rate) is where FAR and FRR are equal. That's the optimal sensitivity setting. Lower CER = better system.
  • Higher sensitivity = FRR ↑, FAR ↓ (more secure, less usable). Lower sensitivity = FRR ↓, FAR ↑. They always move oppositely.
  • Biometric data is permanent: compromise can't be undone by reissuing. GDPR classifies it as a "special category" requiring stronger safeguards.
  • GDPR covers EU residents anywhere. CCPA covers California residents. SHIELD Act is New York, security and breach notification focused.
  • "Right to be forgotten" = GDPR data subject right (deletion).
  • Data sovereignty: data follows the laws of where it's collected AND where it's stored. GDPR applies to EU resident data even if stored on a US server.
quick decision rules
  • Unauthorized user gets through biometric check → False Acceptance Rate (FAR)
  • Legitimate user denied access → False Rejection Rate (FRR)
  • Optimal biometric sensitivity tuning → Crossover Error Rate (CER)
  • "Right to be forgotten" / deletion right → GDPR
  • Right to opt out of data sale → CCPA
  • Reasonable security + breach notification (NY) → SHIELD Act
  • Biometric data classified as "special category" → GDPR
  • EU resident data on a US server → GDPR still applies (data sovereignty)
  • "Data follows the laws of where it's collected/stored" → Data sovereignty
