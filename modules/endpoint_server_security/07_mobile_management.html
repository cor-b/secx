Mobile Management

Securing the devices that leave the building. MDM provides the hands-on technical controls within the broader EMM strategy, from app control to remote wipe and geofencing.

overview

Mobile management centers on Mobile Device Management (MDM), a component of Enterprise Mobility Management (EMM). EMM is the broader suite of policies and tools for managing and securing mobile devices; MDM provides the hands-on technical controls that enforce those policies. MDM secures devices that constantly leave and re-enter the network through features like app control, MFA, patch management, remote wipe, and geofencing.

MDM within EMM

With mobile devices and wearables constantly connecting to external networks and returning to the enterprise, they bring real risk. Enterprise Mobility Management (EMM) is the comprehensive approach combining policies and tools to centrally manage and secure them. Mobile Device Management (MDM) is the key technical component: while EMM covers the broader strategy, MDM delivers the hands-on controls that enforce compliance, manage settings, and protect data across every device.

the six MDM features

Application control
Manage which apps can be installed, configured, blocked, or removed. Example: block social media apps on company smartphones to cut distraction and protect data.
Password / passcode
Enforce strong password policies device-wide or per app, including biometrics. Prevents weak passcodes and applies policy consistently across all devices.
Multi-factor authentication
Require two or more authentication factors, triggered by conditions. MDM can require MFA when a device is detected outside a defined geographic area.
Token-based access
Use digital certificates to authenticate devices before they reach network resources. Often integrated with network access control to confirm a device meets standards before granting access.
Patch management
Manage OS and application updates from a central patch repository. Non-compliant devices can be blocked from the network until they update.
Remote wipe
Remotely erase a lost or stolen device, resetting it to factory settings. Requires the device to be online; to mitigate, MDM can auto-wipe after too many failed passwords or a failed security check.

geofencing and device certificates

Geofencing creates a virtual boundary around a physical area (the company premises) using GPS or RFID. When a device enters or exits the geofenced zone, MDM automatically enforces policies, for example disabling the camera and microphone on site. MDM can also issue user-specific device certificates to uniquely identify each device and revoke access for an individual device when needed.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • MDM is a component of EMM. EMM = broad strategy/policy; MDM = hands-on technical controls.
  • Six MDM features: application control, password/passcode, MFA, token-based access, patch management, remote wipe.
  • Geofencing = virtual boundary via GPS/RFID that auto-enforces policy on enter/exit (e.g. disable camera/mic on premises).
  • Remote wipe requires network connectivity; mitigate with auto-wipe after failed passwords or failed security checks.
  • Token-based access uses device certificates, often integrated with NAC to verify the device before granting access.
decision rules
  • Scenario involves the broad policy strategy for securing mobile devices → EMM
  • Scenario involves the technical controls enforcing those policies on devices → MDM
  • Scenario involves disabling the camera when a phone enters the building → geofencing
  • Scenario involves erasing a lost phone remotely → remote wipe
  • Scenario involves auto-erasing after too many wrong passcodes → auto-wipe (remote wipe mitigation)
  • Scenario involves certificate-based device authentication before network access → token-based access
