Fail-Safe Mechanisms

When something breaks, what does the system default to? Fail-secure locks down to protect data; fail-safe stays open to protect people and keep running.

overview

Fail-safe mechanisms default to a secure or safe state when a failure or breach occurs. Two opposing approaches: fail-secure (prioritizes security, locks down or shuts off access even at the cost of availability) and fail-safe (prioritizes safety/continuity, keeps operating or stays open even if it relaxes some security).

approach 01

Fail-Secure

Security first: lock down/shut off on failure. A vault door that locks on power loss. May cause temporary denial of service.

approach 02

Fail-Safe

Safety/continuity first: keep operating or stay open. An emergency exit that unlocks on power loss. May relax some security.

fail-secure

Fail-secure mechanisms prioritize security: when a failure occurs, the system shuts down or locks access to prevent unauthorized entry or data breaches, even if it temporarily disrupts availability. Example: a server with sensitive data that, on a critical error, cuts off all access until the issue is resolved; or a bank's core database that automatically locks down on an anomaly (an unusual data-request pattern), safeguarding records even though legitimate users are temporarily blocked. The physical analogy: a vault door that locks when power fails, keeping valuables protected.

fail-safe

Fail-safe mechanisms prioritize safety and continuity: the system keeps functioning in a way that prevents harm, even if that means relaxing some security. The physical analogy: an emergency exit that unlocks when power fails, so people can escape a fire, even though it slightly reduces security by allowing outside access. In IT, network switches/routers can be configured so that if the primary internet connection fails, traffic reroutes to a secondary ISP or VPN, even if that backup path has weaker firewall protection, prioritizing business continuity over strict security during failover.

the door analogy

Two doors during a power outage: the fail-secure vault door locks (security first, valuables stay protected); the fail-safe emergency exit unlocks (safety first, people get out). The right choice depends on whether protecting assets or protecting people/operations matters most for that system.

EXAM TIPS

what to know for the exam

  • Fail-secure = security first: lock down/shut off on failure (may cause a temporary denial of service).
  • Fail-safe = safety/continuity first: keep operating or stay open (may relax some security).
  • Power-outage doors: fail-secure vault door LOCKS; fail-safe emergency exit UNLOCKS.
  • Choose fail-secure to protect data/assets; fail-safe to protect people and maintain operations.
  • Rerouting to a less-secure backup link to stay online is a fail-safe behavior.
decision rules
  • Scenario locks a database down on an anomaly, blocking even legit users → fail-secure
  • Scenario unlocks doors during a fire so people exit → fail-safe
  • Scenario reroutes to a weaker backup link to stay online → fail-safe
  • Scenario cuts all access to protect data until fixed → fail-secure
